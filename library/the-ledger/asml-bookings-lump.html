ASML's Bookings Swing Fivefold Between Quarters, and the Market Trades Each One

The sole maker of EUV lithography earns 53% margins on hardware, then reports the demand behind it once a quarter, in order numbers that halve and double.

8 min read2026-07-13

The company chipmakers cannot route around

In the first quarter of 2026 ASML sold €8.8bn of equipment and service at a 53.0% gross margin, and dropped €2.76bn of that to net income, €7.15 per share.1 A capital-equipment vendor earning a software company's margin is doing something the rest of its industry cannot, and the reason is not operational excellence. It is that €6.3bn of the quarter was new machines,2 and at the leading edge every one of those machines is a monopoly product.

Extreme ultraviolet lithography prints the smallest features on an advanced chip by focusing 13.5-nanometre light generated by vaporising tin droplets with a laser. ASML is the only company that sells a working EUV system, and it has been for a decade; a would-be competitor faces a supply chain and an engineering lag measured in years, not quarters, which is why analysts who study the tool call it close to unreplicable.3 TSMC, Samsung, and Intel cannot make a modern logic chip without it, and there is no second vendor to call. That is the whole bull case, and it is correct. The interesting question is what a genuine monopoly is still exposed to, and the answer sits in how the orders arrive.

The machine sale, and the annuity behind it

ASML earns money in two motions. The first is the system sale: a leading-edge EUV tool ships for several hundred million euros,3 and a customer building a new fab buys them in batches. The second is quieter and more durable. Once a tool is installed it needs spare parts, software, and performance upgrades for its whole operating life, and roughly nine of every ten systems ASML has ever built are still running.3 That installed base threw off €2.49bn of management revenue in the first quarter alone,1 a recurring stream that grows with every shipment and does not reverse when a customer pauses new orders.

Over a year the two motions compound into a business that looks serenely stable. ASML reported €32.7bn of net sales for 2025 at a 52.8% gross margin and €9.6bn of net income, and closed the year with a backlog of €38.8bn.4 A €38.8bn order book against €32.7bn of annual sales reads like more than a year of demand already banked. The trouble is that the backlog is a smoothed photograph of something that does not move smoothly at all.

Demand that books in lumps

A fab is a multi-year, multi-billion commitment, and the customer places the equipment order for it in one decision, not in a steady drip. The buyers who can place such an order are few: leading-edge EUV goes to three logic makers, TSMC, Samsung, and Intel, and to the memory makers building advanced DRAM.3 With a customer list that short, a single fab decision swings the whole order line. So ASML's net bookings, the orders it signs in a given quarter, arrive in lumps that bear little relation to the quarter's actual shipments. In the fourth quarter of 2025 the company booked €13.2bn of new orders, €7.4bn of it EUV.4 The bookings line measures when a handful of very large capital decisions happen to close, which is a different thing from the quarter's underlying demand.

The market has not made its peace with this. On 15 October 2024 ASML's quarterly bookings came in at €2.6bn,5 roughly half the analyst consensus near €5.39bn, and the company cut its 2025 sales outlook toward €30–35bn.6 The stock fell more than 16% that day and dragged the broader chip complex down with it.6 Fourteen months later the same line printed €13.2bn.4 Nothing about ASML's monopoly changed across those two prints. What changed was the timing of when a few customers signed, and the market treated a scheduling artifact as a referendum on the health of artificial intelligence demand.

This is the first real exposure of the monopoly. ASML's revenue is durable, but its most-watched number is noise dressed as signal, and a business that reports demand once a quarter in figures that can halve or quintuple is a business whose share price will overshoot in both directions. An investor who buys the €13.2bn print as a trend, or sells the €2.6bn print as a break, is trading the calendar, not the company.

The buyer the export desk is removing

The second exposure is more permanent, because it is written into law rather than a scheduling quirk. In 2024 China was ASML's largest market by a wide margin: 41% of net system sales, ahead of South Korea at 21%, the United States at 17%, and Taiwan at 11%.7 Much of that was deep-ultraviolet immersion equipment, a generation behind EUV, which Chinese fabs bought heavily to build out mature-node capacity that export rules still allowed.

Those rules are closing. From 7 September 2024 the Dutch government required ASML to obtain a license to export its TWINSCAN NXT:1970i and 1980i immersion systems, extending controls that already covered its most advanced immersion tools.8 The effect is a managed decline of the single largest slice of ASML's system revenue. ASML's own CEO flagged that the range of its 2026 guidance is built to absorb "potential outcomes of ongoing discussions around export controls,"1 which is a careful way of saying the China number is a policy variable the company does not control. A monopoly can defend a price. It cannot issue an export license to itself.

Priced to the 2030 line

Set the two exposures against what the stock is asked to believe. At its 2024 investor day ASML laid out a 2030 scenario of €44–60bn in annual revenue at a 56–60% gross margin, underwritten by a double-digit annual growth rate in EUV lithography spending across both logic and memory through the decade.9 That is the number embedded in the multiple: not next quarter's bookings, but a doubling of the business over six years and a further expansion of already-remarkable margins.

The trajectory so far supports the story. Net sales grew from €28.3bn in 20247 to €32.7bn in 2025,4 and in April ASML raised its 2026 outlook to €36–40bn,1 up from the €34–39bn it had guided in January,4 citing customers accelerating capacity plans. The bull can point to a live upgrade cycle and a monopoly on the tool that enables it. The bear does not dispute the monopoly. The bear notes that a business priced for the top of a 2030 scenario is being asked to grow through a legislated decline in its biggest market, while its clearest demand signal is a bookings line that cannot be read from one quarter to the next. The strong version of the bear case is not that ASML is a bad business. It is that a wonderful business bought at a scenario's ceiling can still disappoint the price.

What the next print tests

ASML reports second-quarter results on 15 July, and the number that will move the stock is bookings, not revenue. Revenue for the quarter is largely already shipped from backlog; the CEO has guided €8.4–9.0bn.1 Bookings are the forward read, and the trap is to treat a single print as the answer. A soft number will be sold as the AI buildout cooling, and a strong one bought as the buildout accelerating, when either could be the same few fab decisions landing a quarter early or a quarter late.

The questions that actually matter resolve slower than a quarter. Does the EUV order rate hold as customers digest the capacity they ordered in the 2025 surge, or does a lull follow the way it did into 2025 after the 2024 miss? How fast does the China line fall as the license regime tightens, and what replaces that revenue? A monopoly on the light that prints logic is one of the best assets in the market, and its owner still has to clear a 2030 bar set by its own guidance, on demand it can only observe four times a year. The tape will move on Wednesday's print. Whether the business is worth its price is a slower question, and this quarter answers only a sliver of it.
