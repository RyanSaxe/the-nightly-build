Policy Debate

A signed U.S.-Saudi nuclear deal puts the enrichment question before Congress

Signed on July 22 and now bound for congressional review, the agreement would keep Saudi Arabia in the American reactor orbit while leaving unresolved the question the review turns on, whether it permits uranium enrichment on Saudi soil.

11 min read2026-07-24

The signature that sends the deal to Congress

On July 22, 2026, Energy Secretary Chris Wright and Saudi Energy Minister Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman signed a peaceful nuclear cooperation agreement alongside a bilateral safeguards agreement. The Department of Energy announced it the next day and said the accord "will now be transmitted to Congress for review."1 Such an accord is a Section 123 agreement, named for the part of the Atomic Energy Act, 42 U.S.C. §2153, that governs it. The law lets the United States share civil nuclear technology only under an agreement Congress can examine and reject by joint resolution.2

The review runs on a fixed schedule. The statute gives Congress a consultation period of at least 30 days of continuous session, then 60 more, 90 days in all, before an agreement takes effect.2 Every Section 123 agreement now in force except the one with India is "nonexempt." On that track the deal takes effect at the end of the period unless Congress passes a joint resolution of disapproval, which the President can veto.3 Opponents would then need a two-thirds majority in both chambers to override him. The public record does not yet establish whether this agreement is on that track or on the "exempt" one, where the reverse holds and Congress must vote to approve. Nor is it confirmed that the formal transmittal and the required proliferation assessment have reached the two foreign-affairs committees, the step that starts the count.4

The benchmark Congress has used to judge deals like this is the one it secured from the United Arab Emirates. In its 2009 Section 123 agreement the UAE bound itself by treaty not to enrich uranium or reprocess spent fuel on its own soil. Nonproliferation officials call that commitment the "gold standard."35

The clause the UAE accepted
"The United Arab Emirates shall not possess sensitive nuclear facilities within its territory or otherwise engage in activities within its territory for, or relating to, the enrichment or reprocessing of material ..."5 U.S.-UAE 123 Agreement, Article 7

The gold standard is a policy choice, not a legal command. The Atomic Energy Act sets nine conditions for a 123 agreement, and none requires a partner to forgo enrichment. The Congressional Research Service confirms the statute "does not include such a requirement," and the Obama administration decided in 2013 to judge each partner case by case rather than demand the UAE's terms of all.3 The UAE's own text frames the pledge as its "voluntary decision to forego any enrichment or reprocessing," then makes it binding.5 The kingdom chose the standard and bound itself to it. American law did not force it.

Whether the Saudi agreement meets that standard, bends it, or breaks it is the question, and the answer is contested because the signed text is not public. The administration says the deal permits no enrichment. President Trump said on July 24 that "there will be no enrichment of material," and the Energy Department's announcement, while stressing "the highest standards of ... nonproliferation," gave no detail on enrichment at all.61 Independent reporting describes something narrower than a flat bar. Time reported that the text would allow enrichment in Saudi Arabia only if a two-year joint study finds it "warranted and commercially viable," and that a U.S. rejection would bar the kingdom from enriching for ten years, on its own or with another foreign partner.7 Al Jazeera reported that the Energy Department "did not provide details on enrichment" while "some US media reports have suggested that the deal will allow Saudi Arabia to enrich uranium domestically."8 A conditional path is not a prohibition, and it is not the UAE's clean bar.

Three reference points for the enrichment question: the statute's floor, the standard the UAE accepted, and the terms reported for the Saudi deal.2

The disagreement that follows is not about whether Saudi Arabia may generate nuclear power. Both sides accept that it may. It is about whether the United States should let the agreement leave any opening for enrichment, the step that turns civil fuel into potential weapons material, on the soil of a state whose leaders have said they would build a bomb if Iran did.

The administration and the engagement-first analysts

Locking Saudi Arabia into American reactors under IAEA safeguards buys more nonproliferation than a walkout would, and the Atomic Energy Act never required the kingdom to renounce a right other nations keep.

Grant the hard part at the outset. Enrichment is the step that makes a civil program weapons-relevant, and no safeguard erases that. Its claim is that refusing the deal does not remove the capability. It removes the United States from the room. Saudi Arabia has four vendors already cleared to bid for its first reactors, among them Russia's Rosatom and China National Nuclear Corporation.4 A walkout hands Riyadh to a supplier that asks for less and sees less. Jennifer Gordon of the Atlantic Council frames it that way: "The only question has been whether the kingdom would choose to work with the US and its allies, or whether it would turn to Russia or China."9

The case rests on more than rivalry. Announcing the November 2025 declaration that set up this deal, the White House called the United States and American companies the kingdom's "partners of choice" in a "decades-long, multi-billion-dollar" partnership "consistent with strong nonproliferation standards."10 As a party to the Non-Proliferation Treaty, Saudi Arabia holds what Article IV calls the "inalienable right" to peaceful nuclear energy, and Article III routes the constraint through IAEA safeguards rather than a ban on the fuel cycle.11 The Energy Department paired the agreement with a bilateral safeguards agreement it says meets "the highest standards of ... nonproliferation."1

And the gold standard the critics invoke is a norm, not a rule. The nine statutory conditions do not include a no-enrichment pledge, and the Research Service confirms the Atomic Energy Act "does not include such a requirement."3 To insist on it here, the side argues, is to demand of Riyadh what the law demands of no one.

The Trump administration, standing here because it negotiated and signed the agreement and now defends it to Congress as consistent with nonproliferation.1

The nonproliferation hawks and the gold-standard senators

Enrichment is the one capability that separates a power program from a weapons option, the kingdom's own leaders have tied it to Iran's bomb, and trading the UAE's binding bar for a study invites the region to follow.

This side concedes the cost the administration presses. Refuse the deal on the gold standard, and Riyadh may turn to Moscow or Beijing, and Washington loses the visibility a supplier relationship brings. It accepts that price because of what enrichment is. The centrifuges that spin uranium up to reactor-grade, a few percent of the fissile isotope, are the same ones that reach the ninety percent a weapon needs when run longer. The Atomic Energy Act treats enrichment and reprocessing as the controlled acts, barring U.S.-supplied material from being "reprocessed, enriched or ... otherwise altered" without American approval, and the Non-Proliferation Treaty's Article II binds non-weapon states not to build the bomb.211 The Arms Control Association calls enrichment and reprocessing plainly "two methods of creating fissile material for nuclear weapons."12

The kingdom's own words are the second pillar. In 2018 Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman told CBS that Saudi Arabia "does not want to acquire any nuclear bomb, but without a doubt, if Iran developed a nuclear bomb, we will follow suit as soon as possible."13 The minister who signed this deal, Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman, said in 2019 that the kingdom wanted "the full cycle," including "enriching the uranium."14 Riyadh calls its program peaceful, and both things sit in the record at once: a stated civil purpose and a stated readiness to weaponize if Iran does.4

The third pillar is precedent. The UAE accepted a binding bar; a Saudi deal that leaves even a conditional opening tells every future partner the bar is negotiable. Matthew Kroenig of the Atlantic Council argues it "would be a mistake ... to allow enrichment in Saudi Arabia as part of any deal," since the kingdom "can rely on imported fuel-cycle services like the many other countries around the world with truly peaceful nuclear programs."9 Saudi Arabia's safeguards posture has weakened, not strengthened: it has never concluded the Additional Protocol that gives the IAEA its most intrusive inspections, and in 2024 it rescinded the small-quantities protocol that had limited its reporting.4 Senator Edward Markey has urged the administration to hold out for "a blanket prohibition on enrichment and reprocessing," warning the deal "risks transforming the Middle East into a nuclear Wild West" and that "Congress should use its authority to reject this agreement."15

Senator Edward Markey, standing here because he sits on the reviewing side and has called on Congress to reject a deal that lacks a bar on enrichment.15

The fight is over a fact the record cannot yet check

Strip the deal to what both sides can see, and the disagreement narrows to a fact no one outside the negotiation can yet verify. Does the signed text permit enrichment on Saudi soil, and under what conditions? The administration says no. Independent reporting says a conditional path.67 The instrument that would settle it is not public, and the two accounts have not been reconciled.

Every "no enrichment" statement traces to one principal, the executive branch: the President, the Energy Department, the officials who negotiated the deal.16 The descriptions of a conditional path come from reporters with no stake in it.78 Two arms of the same administration saying the same thing is one voice, not two confirmations. And the one document that would speak for itself, the agreement text with the proliferation assessment the President must send to Congress, has not been read out publicly.4 Kelsey Davenport of the Arms Control Association flagged the ambiguity before the signing, noting it was "unclear" whether an earlier "no enrichment" remark "was referring to the joint declaration or the final ... agreement."16

Settle the fact and the question changes shape. If the text bars enrichment outright, the deal is close to the UAE's and the fight is largely over. If it opens a conditional path, the disagreement becomes a forecast: can IAEA safeguards plus the Additional Protocol hold enrichment on Saudi soil to civil use, or do they leave a weapons option one political decision away? That forecast is harder to make for Saudi Arabia than for most, because the kingdom has no Additional Protocol in force and has just narrowed its reporting rather than widened it.4 Underneath both runs a clash of values the evidence cannot resolve. A sovereign treaty member claims the same fuel-cycle rights others hold; the precedent the United States set when it persuaded the UAE to give those rights up runs the other way.115

What would move an undecided reader is specific and, for once, datable. The deal text, once the administration transmits it with its proliferation assessment, will show whether the enrichment and inspection clauses match the denial or the reporting. The congressional vote, or the absence of one, will show whether the reviewing body treats the terms as the gold standard or a retreat from it. And the verification arrangement Saudi Arabia actually signs with the IAEA, an Additional Protocol or not, will show whether the safeguards the permit case depends on will exist. Until the text is public, the honest statement is the narrow one. The United States has signed a deal that its own negotiators say bars enrichment and independent reporters say does not, and Congress will answer the enrichment question without yet being able to read it.17
