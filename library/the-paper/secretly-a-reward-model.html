The Paper

The Reward Model Is Already in Your Policy

A single change of variables rewrites the RLHF reward in terms of the policy itself, collapsing a three-model reinforcement-learning pipeline into one classification loss, and it now underpins most open post-training.

10 min read2026-07-11

Before you read

This paper assumes three things beyond baseline ML fluency. The first is the standard RLHF pipeline: start from a supervised fine-tuned model, collect human comparisons between pairs of responses, fit a reward model to those comparisons, then use reinforcement learning (usually PPO) to push the policy toward high reward. The second is the KL-constrained objective that RL step actually optimizes: maximize expected reward while penalizing the policy for drifting, in KL-divergence, away from the reference model it started as. The third is the Bradley-Terry model, the standard way to turn a scalar reward into a probability that one response beats another, using a sigmoid of the reward difference. Hold those three and the derivation below has nothing hidden in it.

The pipeline everyone runs, and the cost of running it

Reinforcement learning from human feedback is how a raw language model becomes a usable assistant, and it is the most involved stage in the process that produces one. Direct Preference Optimization starts from a claim about that stage: the reinforcement learning is not necessary to reach the objective it optimizes, and dropping it costs nothing on the tasks tested.1

The recipe DPO is replacing is the one InstructGPT made standard, with three moving parts: supervised fine-tuning, then a separate reward model trained on human preference rankings, then a PPO loop that fine-tunes the policy against that reward while sampling fresh completions at every step.2 The idea of learning a reward from pairwise human comparisons and then optimizing it with RL goes back further, to deep RL from human preferences.3 It works, and it is a pain to run: multiple models trained and served at once, sampling from the policy inside the training loop, and an RL algorithm that is famously touchy to tune.

The objective, before you open it

Treat the RL step as a black box with one job. Given a reward function r(x,y) and a reference policy πref (the supervised model you started from), find the policy that earns the most reward without wandering too far from the reference:

maxπ  E[ r(x,y) ] − β · KL( π(y|x) ‖ πref(y|x) )

The coefficient β sets how hard the KL leash pulls. This is the objective PPO chases by trial and error. But it is not a black box to a theorist: this exact problem has a known closed-form solution, a standard result for KL-regularized objectives that the paper takes from prior work.1 The optimal policy is the reference reweighted by the exponentiated reward:

πr(y|x) = (1 / Z(x)) · πref(y|x) · exp( r(x,y) / β )

Every term here is known except one. The partition function Z(x), the sum of that reweighting over all possible responses y, is intractable for a language model, where the response space is every string it could generate. This is exactly why you cannot just write down the optimal policy and be done. The paper's move is to refuse to compute Z(x) at all.1

Reading the equation backwards

Here is the pivot. That closed-form equation relates a reward to its optimal policy. Read it in the usual direction and it turns a reward into a policy. Read it backwards, solving for the reward, and it turns a policy into a reward. Take the log of both sides and rearrange:

r(x,y) = β · log[ πr(y|x) / πref(y|x) ] + β · log Z(x)

Any reward function can be written in terms of its own optimal policy, the reference policy, and that stubborn partition function.1 The reward is no longer a separate network you have to train. It is a simple expression in the policy you already have. This is the sense in which a language model is secretly a reward model: the ratio of its log-probabilities to the reference's, scaled by β, is an implicit reward.

Implicit reward The quantity β · log[π(y|x) / πref(y|x)]. It is never trained as its own model; it is read off the policy and reference at any point in training. DPO fits it implicitly by optimizing the policy directly.

Why the partition function disappears

The intractable Z(x) is still sitting in that expression, so nothing is won yet. It vanishes because of what the preference model needs. Bradley-Terry says the probability that response yw beats yl depends only on the difference of their rewards, passed through a sigmoid. When you substitute the backwards-read reward into that difference, the β · log Z(x) term is the same for both responses to the same prompt, so it cancels.1 What remains is a preference probability written entirely in policy log-ratios. Turn that into a maximum-likelihood objective over a dataset of preferred and dispreferred pairs and you get the DPO loss:

LDPO = −E[ log σ( β log πθ(yw|x)/πref(yw|x) − β log πθ(yl|x)/πref(yl|x) ) ]

This is a binary cross-entropy loss. It has no reward model, no sampling from the policy, and no RL. You compute four log-probabilities per example, the policy and reference on the preferred and dispreferred response, and take a gradient step. That is the entire algorithm.1

The obvious worry is that this collapses into a trivial objective that just raises π(yw) and lowers π(yl) until the model degenerates. It does not, because of the weight the sigmoid puts on each example. Reading the gradient shows what the update is really doing.

The paper reports that a naive version without this weighting does let the model degenerate, which is the empirical case for keeping it.1 The KL constraint that PPO enforces with an explicit penalty is here baked into the shape of the loss.

What the experiments actually measured

A clean derivation is not a result. The paper backs it with three tasks. On controlled sentiment generation, where the true reward is known and the reward-versus-KL frontier can be drawn exactly, DPO sits on or above PPO's frontier at every KL value, meaning it earns more reward for the same drift from the reference.1 On Reddit TL;DR summarization, judged by GPT-4 against reference summaries, DPO wins about 61% of the time at temperature 0 against PPO's 57% at its best temperature, and it holds up far better as sampling temperature rises.1 The summarization setup and dataset come straight from the earlier learning-to-summarize work.4

In a head-to-head human evaluation, DPO samples were preferred 58% of the time over PPO samples.1 And on an out-of-distribution shift, moving the TL;DR-trained policies to CNN/DailyMail news articles, DPO kept its edge, winning 0.36 versus PPO's 0.26 at temperature 0, initial evidence that the reward-free policy generalizes about as well as the RL-trained one.1

Read as a reviewer, the limits are as important as the wins. Every model here is 6 billion parameters or smaller; the paper is explicit that scaling DPO to models orders of magnitude larger was untested and left to future work.1 The summarization and dialogue win rates lean on GPT-4 as the judge, which the authors partly defend by showing GPT-4 agrees with humans about as often as humans agree with each other, but a judge is still a judge.1 The dialogue evaluation is single-turn. None of this sinks the result; it bounds it. What was shown is that at this scale, on these tasks, a classification loss matches or beats a tuned PPO pipeline.

What holds up
  • The derivation is exact, not an approximation; the same KL-constrained objective is optimized.
  • No reward model, no sampling in the loop, little hyperparameter tuning.
  • Matches or beats PPO on every task tried, including under distribution shift.
What to be careful about
  • Largest model is 6B; frontier-scale behavior was not tested here.
  • Most win rates depend on an LLM judge and single-turn tasks.
  • The offline loss assumes fixed preference data; it never sees its own new samples.

What the field did with it

DPO landed on the exact pain point practitioners felt, and adoption was fast. Zephyr-7B used a distilled variant, dDPO, on AI-ranked preferences and reported beating the best 70B RLHF chat model of its day on MT-Bench, a concrete demonstration that the reward-free recipe scaled to a competitive open model.5 Within a year, direct preference methods were the default first choice for open post-training.

The idea did not go unchallenged. The theoretical critique came from the ΨPO framework, which casts RLHF and DPO as special cases of one objective and identifies a specific failure mode: because DPO inherits the Bradley-Terry assumption, deterministic or near-deterministic preferences push it to drive log-ratios to extremes, and the KL regularization stops biting.6

The empirical pushback was blunter. A comprehensive PPO-versus-DPO study argued that DPO's advantage is partly an artifact of weak PPO baselines, and that a well-implemented PPO surpasses DPO across tasks, reaching state-of-the-art on hard problems like code competitions.7 The honest reading is not that one method won. It is that the two optimize the same objective by different routes, and which route wins depends on implementation quality and how far off-distribution the policy is allowed to move.

Two lines of work extended the idea rather than disputing it. SimPO stripped out the reference model entirely, using the average log-probability of a sequence as the implicit reward and adding a target margin, and reported gains over DPO on AlpacaEval 2 and Arena-Hard.8 And a follow-up from two of DPO's own authors showed the loss can be re-derived in the token-level MDP as an inverse Q-learning algorithm, giving the single sequence-level score a credit-assignment reading across tokens.9 The change of variables that started as a shortcut turned out to have more structure than a shortcut needs.

Read it yourself

The paper is unusually readable, and the derivation is short. A full read adds the exact algebra this reconstruction compressed and the theoretical framing that connects DPO to actor-critic methods.

  • read §4 Direct Preference Optimization four equations carry the whole idea; derive Eq. 5 from Eq. 4 with a pen.
  • read §5.1 Your Language Model Is Secretly a Reward Model the equivalence-class argument that makes the reparameterization rigorous.
  • skim §6 Experiments Figure 2 is the one to stare at: reward-versus-KL frontier and temperature robustness.
  • skip §2 Related Work standard coverage; this article already placed the paper among its neighbors.

The thing first-time readers miss: DPO never instantiates the reward. The implicit reward is a lens for reading the loss, not a model that gets trained. If you find yourself looking for where the reward network lives, that is the point being made.
