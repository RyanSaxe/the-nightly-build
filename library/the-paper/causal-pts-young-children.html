Four Direct Causes of Childhood PTSD, Read Off an Observational Graph

On 1,354 maltreated children, a causal-discovery pipeline built a 251-node graph and pulled the direct causes and effects of post-traumatic stress off it, including that the stress itself raises the risk of a later assault.

Posttraumatic stress in young children at risk for maltreatment: a causal data science analysis

Glenn N. Saxe, Leah J. Morales, Sisi Ma, Mehmet Urgurbil, Constantin Aliferis · Frontiers in Health Services 6:1784459 · 2026

Read the paper
This article features the application of Causal Data Science (CDS) methods to determine the mechanism for Posttraumatic Stress (PTS) in young, maltreated children, in order to advance knowledge for prevention. Advances in prevention require research that identifies causal factors, but the scientific literature that would inform the identification of causes are almost exclusively based on the application of correlational methods to observational data. Causal inferences from such research will frequently be in error. We conducted the present study to explore the application of CDS methods as an alternative—or a supplement—to experimental methods, which can rarely be applied in human research on causal factors for PTS. A data processing pipeline that integrates state-of-the-art CDS algorithms was applied to an existing observational, longitudinal data set collected by the Consortium for Longitudinal Studies in Child Abuse and Neglect (LONGSCAN). This data set contains a sample of 1,354 children who were identified in infancy to early childhood as being maltreated or at risk. A causal network model of 251 variables (nodes) and 818 bivariate relations (edges) was discovered, revealing four direct causes (Emotional Maltreatment at age 0–4, Physical Assault at age 8, Feeling of Safety at age 8, and Witnessing Violence at age 8) and two direct effects (Negative Self-Image and Severe Assault from a Non-Caregiver at age 8) of PTS at age 8, within a network containing a broad diversity of causal pathways. These results indicate that CDS methods show promise for research on the complex etiology of PTS in young, maltreated children.1

Before you read

The machinery here is the machinery of causal graphs, and the paper assumes you can read one: a directed edge as a direct cause, a path as a chain of influence, and the fact that conditioning on the right variable can make a correlation vanish. What it does not assume, and what a machine-learning reader usually lacks, is the clinical vocabulary. Maltreatment is coded here in subtypes (physical, emotional, neglect, and witnessing violence). Post-traumatic stress, abbreviated PTS throughout, is a measured symptom load in an eight-year-old, not a formal diagnosis. And the sample is not the general population: every child was flagged in infancy as maltreated or at risk. If you can read a directed acyclic graph and you take on trust that these constructs come from validated instruments, you have what you need.

The prevention question that observation cannot answer

Preventing a disorder means acting on its causes, so prevention research lives or dies on knowing what those causes are. For post-traumatic stress in maltreated children the stakes are not small. A meta-analysis of trauma-exposed preschoolers puts the pooled rate of PTSD at 21.5% under developmentally appropriate criteria, roughly threefold higher after interpersonal and repeated trauma than after a single non-interpersonal event, and it notes that scoring young children against adult criteria collapses the estimate to 4.9% and hides most cases.2 The disorder is common, it is under-counted, and the children who carry it are exactly the ones prevention should reach.

The trap is that almost everything known about its causes comes from correlational studies of observational data, and the paper is blunt that this is a problem: causal inferences drawn that way will frequently be in error.1 The clean answer, a randomized experiment, is barred here for the obvious reason that no one may assign a child to be maltreated. So the field is stuck between the evidence it can ethically collect and the causal claims it actually needs. The paper's move is to take the observational data it already has and run it through a causal-discovery pipeline that recovers a causal graph, then read the direct causes and direct effects of PTS off the neighborhood of that graph around the PTS node.1

From a conditional independence to a causal edge

The idea that observation can yield causation at all rests on two assumptions that connect a probability distribution to a graph. The first is the Causal Markov Condition: a variable is independent of its non-effects once you condition on its direct causes. The second is Faithfulness, its converse: every independence in the data is one the graph actually requires, so no independence is an accident of coincidentally cancelling paths.3 The paper leans on a plain instance of the first: if variable B sits between A and C, then conditioning on B breaks their association.

Screening off Conditioning on the variables that mediate between two others removes the statistical dependence between them. A direct edge is the dependence that no conditioning set can remove.

Put the two assumptions together and the data stops being a fog of correlations. It becomes a faithful image of the graph that generated it.

The definition of cause underneath this is interventionist: X is a direct cause of Y if setting X by an intervention that overrides its own causes still changes Y, holding the other variables fixed.3 An indirect cause loses that power once you fix the mediators between it and the effect. Constraint-based discovery algorithms such as PC turn these assumptions into a procedure: test conditional independences, draw an edge between two variables exactly when no conditioning set makes them independent, then orient what can be oriented.4 The honest limit is that observation alone rarely fixes every arrow. It recovers a Markov equivalence class, the set of graphs sharing the same independences, and only colliders, the v-structures where two arrows meet at a childless node, orient unambiguously from the data.4

Looking locally, then building the whole web

Recovering an entire graph over hundreds of variables is expensive and statistically fragile. But the question here is narrow: what causes and what is caused by PTS. That neighborhood has a name in the theory, and it is exactly the answer the study wants.

That structural reading is what makes local discovery worth the trouble. The Markov boundary of PTS holds its direct causes, its direct effects, and the co-parents of those effects, so recovering it answers the causal question without recovering the rest of the graph.1 The paper's edge detection comes from the Generalized Local Learning family, algorithms built to induce exactly these local structures, direct causes and effects and Markov blankets, around a target in high-dimensional data with modest sample sizes.5

Working locally does not mean ignoring the rest. PTS has what the authors call a complex etiology: many interacting pathways rather than one switch. Their prior work reframed psychiatric causation as a complex system, where influence is concentrated in a few high-traffic nodes rather than spread evenly.6

So the design has two layers. Discover a global causal network over all the variables, then read the local Markov boundary of PTS off it for the direct causes and effects, and separately count how much of the network each node's influence flows through.

The cohort and the pipeline

The data is LONGSCAN, a consortium study that followed children identified as maltreated or at risk for maltreatment across five United States sites, assessing them every two years from age four into adolescence.7 From its 1,354 children the study drew on the age 4, 6, and 8 waves and measured PTS at age 8.1

The pipeline is a sequence of deliberate reductions. Two hundred fifty-two variables were pre-selected for their plausibility as causes or effects of PTS, and the analysis ran on the 1,053 children with complete PTS data. Edge detection used the GLL-PC algorithm with a maximum conditioning-set size of three and a p-value threshold of 0.05, and the PC algorithm's orientation rules turned undirected adjacencies into arrows wherever colliders allowed. The result was a global causal network of 251 nodes and 818 edges.1

Two safeguards matter for reading the results. The data subset was bootstrapped one hundred times and a network rebuilt on each resample, so every edge carries a stability score: the fraction of the hundred networks in which it appeared. And a multiplicity test using the TIE* algorithm checked that the data supported a single Markov boundary for the target rather than several competing ones.1 Missing values were handled by test-wise deletion inside the modeling rather than by imputation. These are the guards against reading noise as structure, and the stability score is the number to hold onto when the findings arrive.

What the graph says about PTS

The local model around PTS at age 8 is small enough to state in full. Four arrows point in.

Read them together and the shape is coherent. Emotional maltreatment recorded in the first four years reaches forward to symptoms at eight, which is a claim about early insult and delayed cost. Being physically assaulted and witnessing violence at eight act in the present. And a child's own sense of safety enters as a direct protective cause: in this model it lowers the symptom load, which makes it the kind of node an intervention could target without first ending the maltreatment.1

The two arrows pointing out of PTS are where the graph earns its keep, because a correlational study cannot tell an effect from a cause and here the direction is the finding. One effect, a negative self-image, is unsurprising. The other is not: severe assault by a non-caregiver at age eight is a downstream effect of PTS, not a cause of it.1

It is tempting to read every association with PTS as a road into the disorder. This arrow runs out of it. A purely observational dataset, run through the right machinery, oriented an edge that the correlational literature could only have left as a two-way association.

Beyond the immediate neighborhood, the study ranked influence by counting pathways. Using a path-enumeration tool that finds the short paths connecting nodes in a network,8 the authors traced every path of four steps or fewer that ended at PTS.

How much of this to believe

Weighed as a reviewer would weigh it, the contribution is real and the caveats are specific. On the credit side, the graph does something a correlation table cannot: it excludes confounding by every measured variable in the analysis. The authors state the scope of that guarantee precisely.

That boundary is the hinge the whole causal reading hangs on. The machinery assumes causal sufficiency, meaning no unmeasured common cause of two variables in the set. A hidden factor driving both a child's feeling of safety and their symptoms would masquerade as the direct edge the model reports, and no amount of measured-variable adjustment catches it. Faithfulness can fail too, when real causal paths cancel and hide a dependence the graph should show. And many edges are bootstrap-unstable, appearing in a minority of resamples, which flags them for replication rather than trust. The authors close on the caution this warrants.

What holds up
  • Edges are unconfounded by every one of the 252 measured variables, a guarantee no correlational analysis supplies.
  • The two arrows out of PTS give directions, including the counterintuitive assault effect, that observation usually leaves undirected.
  • Bootstrap stability and multiplicity testing are built in, so the uncertainty on each edge is reported rather than hidden.
What to be careful about
  • Causal sufficiency is assumed: an unmeasured common cause can still masquerade as a direct edge.
  • Faithfulness can fail, and low-stability edges need replication before they carry weight.
  • One cohort, one measurement age, and methods rarely used in the field: the specific edges are hypotheses, not settled etiology.
Verdict The durable contribution is a method, not a map of what causes childhood PTS. The paper shows that a developmental cohort collected for other purposes can be run through causal discovery to yield oriented, testable causal hypotheses where an experiment is impossible and a correlation is uninformative.4 Take the pipeline as the transferable result and each individual edge as a candidate awaiting replication, with the causal-sufficiency assumption as the first thing a follow-up should stress-test.1

Read it yourself

A full read adds the two things this reconstruction compressed: the formal statement of the assumptions that license the whole enterprise, and the exact variable definitions behind the named causes and effects.

  • read §2.1 Causal inference from observational data: the Markov condition, faithfulness, and the Markov boundary in the authors' own words. If you know constraint-based discovery this is a fast confirmation; if you do not, it is the primer.
  • read §3.1 Direct causes and effects of PTS: the local network figure. Seeing the four causes and two effects as a single small graph fixes the result better than the prose can.
  • skim §2.3 Methods: pull the specific settings, GLL-PC with max-k of three at p 0.05, the hundred bootstraps, and the TIE* multiplicity test, then move on.
  • skim §3.2 Paths influencing PTS: note which variables sit on the most paths, but treat the exact path counts as descriptive of this one network.
  • skip §2.3.4 Software and tools: the MATLAB and Cytoscape specifics matter only if you intend to rerun the pipeline yourself.

The one point worth carrying out of the paper is the boundary on its own claim. Its edges are clean of confounding by anything it measured, and silent about anything it did not.
