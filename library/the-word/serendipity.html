The Word

Horace Walpole built ‘serendipity’ to require sagacity, not luck

He built it in a 1754 letter out of a fairy tale about three traveling princes, and fixed the rule himself: a discovery you set out to make does not count.

5 min read2026-07-21
Serendipity

serendipity, n.: the faculty of finding something of value while looking for something else, where the finding takes both an accident and the sagacity to see what the accident has turned up. Horace Walpole made the word in a letter dated 28 January 1754.1

The word arrives whole, in a letter to Florence

Walpole was writing from London to Sir Horace Mann, his longtime correspondent in Florence, about a discovery he had just made about a portrait. “This discovery, indeed, is almost of that kind which I call Serendipity, a very expressive word,” he wrote.1 The word appears only this once in everything Walpole wrote; he coined it in a private letter and never put it into print.2

The derivation was a fairy tale, misremembered

Walpole had read, he told Mann, “a silly fairy tale, called the three Princes of Serendip.”1 Serendip is the old Persian name for Sri Lanka, then Ceylon.3 He read it in an English translation printed in London in 1722, carried from the Persian by way of French, not in the original.2 He handed Mann the sharpest instance: “one of them discovered that a mule blind of the right eye had travelled the same road lately, because the grass was eaten only on the left side, where it was worse than on the right.”1 The animal in the story is in fact a camel. Robert Merton and Elinor Barber, in their history of the word, note that Walpole’s “memory had transformed the camel of the original story into a mule.”2

The tale gave him a method more than a match. Its princes “found nothing at all, but merely gave repeated evidence of their powers of observation,” so that the story, in Merton and Barber’s judgment, “scarcely resembles Walpole’s account of it.”2 The word he drew out is more exact than the source that seeded it.

Walpole wrote the rule into the word

The definition, verbatim
“As their Highnesses travelled, they were always making discoveries, by accidents and sagacity, of things which they were not in quest of … One of the most remarkable instances of this accidental sagacity (for you must observe that no discovery of a thing you are looking for comes under this description)…”1 Horace Walpole to Sir Horace Mann, 28 January 1754

Two conditions, both required. There must be an accident, a thing not in quest of, and the sagacity to catch what it turns up. The phrase is “accidents and sagacity,” the two held together, not one or the other. Walpole then drew the boundary inside the parenthesis: a discovery of a thing you are looking for does not come under the description. He built the word to name a joint, not a stroke of luck. As Merton and Barber put it, “it may be that, of the two, Walpole preferred to accent sagacity rather than accident.”2

Cannon carried it into science and misquoted Walpole

The word reentered wide use through science. In 1945 the Harvard physiologist Walter B. Cannon, who had already given medicine homeostasis, titled a chapter of his memoir “Gains from Serendipity” and made the word a term of art for how research stumbles onto what it was not seeking.4 In the same passage he wore the meaning down. Cannon quoted Walpole as writing “by accident or sagacity” where the letter reads “by accidents and sagacity,” then reported that in dictionaries the word “is said to designate the happy faculty, or luck, of finding unforeseen evidence of one’s ideas.”4 The misquotation drops the plural and swaps and for or, and the dictionary gloss adds luck: and makes sagacity a partner of accident, or makes it an alternative, and luck drops it. Walpole’s condition was quietly written out. Cannon did not lose it completely; his chapter closes on “chances of serendipity which the sagacious can utilize.”4

Nearly silent for a century, then everywhere

Having coined it once, Walpole let it sit, and for roughly 120 years it stayed almost unused.25 The revival came late in the nineteenth century, and it arrived already bent: by 1877 a newspaper could use the word for finding exactly what one was after, the reverse of Walpole’s stipulation.5 Even the adjective is young: Merriam-Webster finds “no evidence of the adjectival form of the word, serendipitous, in use until 1910, when it appeared in a book on fishing.”6 A word coined in 1754 did nearly all of its living in the twentieth century.

Sagacity survives the drift

Serendipity as a plain happy accident is now the common sense, but the fuller meaning did not die with Walpole’s condition. It holds most firmly where discovery is the daily work. Writing on accidental breakthroughs in science in 2024, Supriya Subramanian defined the term as “an unexpected discovery due to a chance encounter (not intentionally looking for it), sagacity (wisdom and scientific knowledge required to understand the chance observation), and a prepared mind…”7 That is Walpole’s structure intact: accident and sagacity both named, the parenthesis restored.

What the word keeps, and what the loose sense spends, is that second condition. Luck names the accident alone, asking nothing of the person it lands on. Serendipity names the accident together with the mind able to read it, and the two cannot be pulled apart, because Walpole ruled out the case where only the accident is present. A discovery made on purpose cannot be serendipitous: the accident is there in any unplanned result, but without the sagacity to see what it offers, nothing is found.1 That is the faculty Walpole made a word for, and the part worth keeping.
