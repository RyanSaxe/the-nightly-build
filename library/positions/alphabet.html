Alphabet: The Ad Utility That Is Funding Its Own Reinvention

Advertising still throws off nearly three-quarters of Alphabet's revenue — and that cash is quietly paying for a $91 billion AI build that most rivals have to borrow to match.

7 min read2026-07-14

Alphabet is three businesses stacked on one cash machine. Google Services — Search, YouTube, the ad network, Android and the devices — is the machine. Google Cloud is the challenger that has finally turned profitable. Other Bets is the rounding error that occasionally matters. In the year to December 2025 the group booked $402.8bn of revenue, its first year above $400bn, up 15 per cent, and about $294.7bn of that — roughly 73 per cent — was advertising.12

The reason the company is interesting right now is the tension inside those numbers. The advertising utility is throwing off enough profit — $129.0bn of operating income at a 32 per cent margin in 2025 — to self-fund an infrastructure build most rivals must raise money for, while the same artificial intelligence that build is meant to win is also the clearest long-term threat to the search box that pays for it.1 This piece is about whether the moat still holds, quantified: what protects the ad engine, how real Cloud's profitability is, and what $91bn of capex is actually buying.

The utility nobody has dislodged

The moat is distribution at a scale no competitor can price against. Google Services generated $342.7bn of revenue in 2025 and $139.4bn of operating income — a 40.7 per cent operating margin on the largest advertising business ever assembled.3 Search and its adjacent properties alone brought in $224.5bn, up 13.4 per cent on the year; YouTube's advertising added $40.4bn and the Network business $29.8bn.2

Why the margin persists An advertising auction has almost no marginal cost: the incremental query is nearly free to serve, and the incremental advertiser bids against every other advertiser for the same intent. Scale widens the auction, which raises the clearing price, which funds the distribution that sustains the scale. That loop, not any single product, is the moat.

The delivery mechanism is distribution, which the filings describe plainly: Alphabet's products and platforms — Search, Chrome, Android, Maps, Play and YouTube — each reach billions of users, and that default placement is what converts a better auction into realised revenue.5 This is the part AI challengers have not solved. A rival can build a better answer engine; getting it in front of the query, on the phone and in the browser the user already holds, is the harder problem, and it is the one the search box still owns.

The standing threat to the utility is not only technological but legal. The 10-K's own risk factors flag "increasingly heightened scrutiny from both US and foreign governments" over competition and consumer protection alongside AI — the antitrust overhang on the ad business is disclosed in the filing, not merely in the headlines, and it is the one force that could reshape the distribution loop from the outside rather than compete with it head-on.8

The number to watch is not the level but the growth: a business this size still compounding search revenue at double digits is the strongest evidence that the much-forecast collapse of the search franchise has not begun. Management is candid that it could — the 10-K warns plainly that competitors "may develop AI products and services" that are "similar or superior," and that there is "no assurance that we will adapt effectively" to shifts in how people find information.8 The cash machine is intact; the risk is real; the numbers have not broken.

The second engine finally earns its keep

For years Google Cloud was the segment you excused. That excuse is gone. Cloud's operating margin has gone from 5.2 per cent in 2023 to 14.1 per cent in 2024 to 23.7 per cent in 2025 — an operating income of $13.9bn last year, more than double the $6.1bn it earned the year before, on revenue of $58.7bn.3 Then the first quarter of 2026 accelerated again: Cloud revenue of $20.0bn, up 63 per cent year on year, at a 32.9 per cent segment margin.4

This matters for the moat thesis because it changes what Alphabet is. A company whose only profit pool is advertising is one disruption away from trouble; a company with a second, fast-growing, now-profitable infrastructure business has optionality the ad-only version never had. The whole group's first-quarter revenue reached $109.9bn, up 22 per cent, with operating income of $39.7bn at a 36 per cent margin — the operating figure worth anchoring on, because reported net income that quarter was flattered by mark-to-market gains on equity investments rather than the operating business.9

What $91 billion is buying

The defining decision of Alphabet's current chapter is the capital spend. Purchases of property and equipment reached $91.4bn in 2025, up 74 per cent from $52.5bn the year before, and the pace is still climbing: $35.7bn in the first quarter of 2026 alone, more than double the year-earlier quarter and roughly 78 per cent of the $45.8bn of operating cash flow the company generated in the same three months.36

The spend funds a genuinely full-stack position, which is the part of the moat rivals find hardest to copy. Alphabet designs its own AI accelerators — the seventh-generation Tensor Processing Unit, "Ironwood" — runs its own data centres, trains its own frontier models in the Gemini line, and distributes them across products with billions of users.5 Owning the silicon and the data centres is why Cloud's margins can rise while the build accelerates: the capacity it sells is capacity it built for itself. The company also carries $242.8bn of remaining performance obligations — contracted Cloud revenue not yet recognised — about half of it expected within two years, which is the closest thing to a forward order book the filings disclose.7

The capacity Alphabet sells is capacity it built for itself; that is why the margin can climb while the spending does.

The honest caveat is return on that capital. Capex near 80 per cent of operating cash flow is a bet that AI demand converts into Cloud revenue and better ads faster than the depreciation lands. The order book and the margin trend say it is working so far; a build this large is still the single line most exposed if demand slips, and the company itself notes it is now "entering into significant leasing arrangements" to add capacity it cannot build fast enough.8

The tape

Walled off from the business and secondary to it: Alphabet's Class A shares trade on the Nasdaq Global Select Market as GOOGL and its Class C shares as GOOG, and the live quote is worth reading against the fundamentals above rather than on its own.10 What the market is actually pricing is not whether the ad utility works — the filings settle that — but two open questions: whether Cloud's margin can keep climbing off a 63 per cent growth quarter,4 and whether a capex line that hit $35.7bn in a single quarter earns its return before AI erodes the search franchise that funds it.6 The business is the story; the tape is the footnote.

Go deeper

Every number here is free on SEC EDGAR, and the primary documents are more legible than any summary of them. Start with the FY2025 Form 10-K: the segment note reconciles Services, Cloud and Other Bets and carries the Cloud margin history,3 the revenue-by-type table breaks advertising into Search, YouTube and Network,2 and Item 1A is where management states, in its own words, the AI-to-Search and regulatory risks the headlines paraphrase.8 Then read the latest 10-Q for the quarterly trend that the annual figures smooth over — Cloud's acceleration and the capex ramp are both clearest there.9 The one number to track next quarter is Cloud's segment margin: if it keeps rising while capex climbs, the self-funding thesis holds; if it stalls, the build gets harder to justify.
