Does Sleep Training Harm Your Baby?

The claim that letting a baby cry floods the brain with stress hormones and damages attachment rests on one tiny study with no control group; the randomized trials that measured both found neither.

6 min read2026-07-14

Two claims that are not the same size

If you have a baby who wakes six times a night and you have gone looking for whether to sleep train, you have met two claims that seem to cancel out. One says sleep training spikes a baby's cortisol and damages the parent-child bond. The other says it is safe and it works. Presented side by side they look like a wash, a matter of which expert you trust. They are not a wash. One rests on a single study of twenty-five infants with no comparison group; the other rests on randomized trials that followed children for months and years.12 The job of this piece is to weigh them by the strength of their evidence, not the volume of their claims, and to land on what that leaves a parent free to do.

What "sleep training" actually refers to

The phrase covers several methods, and the distinction matters because the evidence is about specific ones. Graduated extinction, the method most studies test, has a parent let the baby settle with checks at increasing intervals rather than responding to every cry. Bedtime fading moves bedtime later to build sleep pressure so the child falls asleep faster with less crying. Neither is the cry-it-out-forever caricature the debate runs on, and the trials that test them enroll babies who are generally six months or older, not newborns.3

Randomized vs correlational A randomized controlled trial assigns families to a method or a control group by chance, so the groups differ only in the treatment; a correlational study observes families who already chose, and cannot rule out that the choice and the outcome share a cause. Almost the entire reassuring case here is randomized. The alarming case is not.

Does it work? The best-supported part

Start with efficacy, because it is where the evidence is strongest and least contested. A review for the American Academy of Sleep Medicine covering fifty-two studies found that ninety-four percent reported behavioral methods effective, with more than eighty percent of treated children showing clinically significant improvement that held at three to six months.4 A randomized trial of controlled crying in 156 families found sleep problems resolved in 53 of 76 treated infants against 36 of 76 controls at two months, and among mothers who had scored high for depression, symptoms fell markedly more in the treated group.5

The effect sizes are real but worth holding at their true weight. A meta-analysis pooling sixteen controlled trials and twelve within-subject studies, roughly 2,560 children, found significant improvements in how fast children fell asleep and how often they woke, which its authors graded as "moderate-level" evidence rather than decisive.6 A newer meta-analysis of ten randomized trials found behavioral methods reduced child sleep problems (odds ratio 0.51) and improved mothers' own sleep, while the effects on infant night awakenings and on maternal depression were not statistically significant.7 The honest summary: it reliably helps children settle and helps exhausted parents, with gains that are solid but moderate, not miraculous.

The cortisol claim, taken apart

The frightening version of the anti-sleep-training case is physiological: the baby stops crying but the stress goes on inside. It comes almost entirely from one 2012 study. Twenty-five infants in an inpatient program underwent five days of extinction; by the third night they cried less, but their cortisol stayed elevated and stopped tracking their mothers'.1 The study is cited as proof of hidden harm. It cannot bear that weight, and the reasons are basic: it had no control group, so there is nothing to compare the cortisol against; it took no baseline showing the cortisol was even elevated to begin with; and it followed twenty-five babies in a hospital, not at home.

The stronger test is the one the alarming study lacked. When the 2016 randomized trial actually measured infant cortisol, it moved in the opposite direction: afternoon cortisol fell in the graduated-extinction group, with a moderate-to-large effect, and fell more than in controls.2 The fair caveat is that this sampling was twice daily, so it could miss a brief spike during a crying episode. But a randomized trial that measured the hormone and found it going down is simply better evidence than an uncontrolled case series that assumed it was going up.

Attachment now, and the child at five

The other fear is about the bond. The same 2016 trial assessed attachment security at twelve months using the standard laboratory measure and found no significant difference between the sleep-trained groups and controls, and no difference in behavioral problems.8 The honest asterisk is small numbers: with a few dozen children per arm, the trial had limited power to detect a modest effect, so read this as "no harm detected," not "harm ruled out to the last decimal."

The longest look comes from a separate randomized trial whose families were followed to age six. On every measure the researchers checked, and there were many, the sleep-trained children were indistinguishable from controls: emotional and conduct problems, sleep, closeness and conflict with parents, chronic stress, and the mothers' own depression all came out even.9 The same result carries a quieter lesson for the other side of the debate: the benefits had also washed out by age six. Sleep training is a tool for the hard months, not a lasting edge.

Where the reassurance stops: under six months

The evidence above earns its confidence for babies past roughly six months. Below that age it does not transfer, and the honest reading flips. A critical review of interventions in the first six months of life found they did not improve infant or maternal outcomes and flagged plausible harms, including more crying, earlier cessation of breastfeeding, and worse maternal anxiety.10 Those harms are hypothesized rather than demonstrated in trials, but the point stands: the case for sleep training is a case about older infants, and the newborn is a different question the evidence answers with "wait."

What this leaves you free to do

For a baby past six months whose sleep is genuinely hurting the household, the fear that sleep training damages attachment or floods the brain with stress hormones is not supported, and the one study that fear leans on cannot carry it.1 The trial that measured cortisol found it fell; attachment at one year and functioning at five looked the same as in children who were not trained.29 What sleep training reliably buys is faster settling, fewer wakings, and real relief for a depleted parent, gains that fade by early childhood in both directions.

Two cautions keep that calibrated. The reassuring trials are small, so "no difference found" partly reflects limited power rather than a guarantee of zero effect; and none of it licenses sleep training a baby under six months, where the evidence says wait. Inside those bounds the decision is yours to make on your family's terms. If crying-based methods feel wrong to you, that preference is legitimate, and the data give you room to honor it: the long-run outcome looks the same either way, which means the choice can rest on what your household can bear, not on a fear of doing damage.
