Zelensky remakes his cabinet as oil spikes on a strait Iran says is shut

Kyiv reshuffles around foreign policy and Brent tops $79 on the Hormuz standoff, while new work pins memory on a protein, stages black-hole physics on a bench, and finds the deep sea feeding itself.

world

Zelensky replaces his prime minister in a wartime cabinet overhaul1

On July 12 President Volodymyr Zelensky announced that Prime Minister Yulia Svyrydenko, a year into the job after brokering the US–Ukraine minerals deal, will step down, in a reshuffle that also reaches the top ranks of the law-enforcement agencies. Zelensky said each foreign-policy priority, from Patriot production to EU accession to closer Gulf ties, would be handed to a named official with the experience to deliver it, and offered Svyrydenko a diplomatic brief with a “key partner.”

Why it matters: it is the fourth reorganisation of Ukraine's government since the full-scale invasion, and the first built around foreign-policy portfolios rather than domestic ones. A cabinet reshaped to lock in air-defence manufacturing and court new partners signals where Kyiv now thinks the war is decided, as it braces for wavering US support.

Oil jumps 4% as America and Iran trade strikes over Hormuz2

Brent crude rose more than 4% on Monday to about $79 a barrel, its highest since June 22, after the US struck Iran a fourth time over the weekend and Iran fired missiles at five Gulf states. Tehran again declared the Strait of Hormuz closed, a claim Washington rejected. Traffic shows the strain: roughly six vessels crossed the strait in one recent overnight window, against a pre-war average near 130 a day.

Why it matters: about a fifth of the world's seaborne oil moves through Hormuz, so even a contested, part-time closure keeps a risk premium in every barrel. The jump reverses crude's recent slide back toward pre-war levels and feeds straight into the inflation the Fed flagged last week.

health & science

Tau, the Alzheimer's suspect, turns out to fix memories in place3

A team led by Flinders University reported in Nature Communications on July 12 that tau, the protein whose tangles mark Alzheimer's, is needed to turn new experiences into lasting memories. In mice, tau carrying a chemical tag added during learning helps select which neurons, the “engram cells,” store a memory and quiets background activity so the trace holds. Tau proved dispensable for immediate learning, and necessary only for recall days later.

Why it matters: it complicates tau's billing as a pure villain and names a mechanism: the same protein that stabilises a memory may corrupt that process when it is mis-modified. Any therapy that strips tau to slow dementia will have to reckon with the work it does when healthy. In mice, so far.

wildcard

Physicists pull energy from a black hole they never had to spin4

Researchers at the CUNY Advanced Science Research Center reported in Nature on July 12 that they reproduced the Penrose–Zel'dovich effect, by which waves steal energy from a rotating black hole, using a ring of radio-frequency resonators whose properties are switched in a synchronised travelling pattern to imitate rotation faster than any object could physically spin. Waves with the right twist came out amplified, drawing energy from the device.

Why it matters: it turns a 50-year-old thought experiment into a bench-top system with no black hole and no moving part, so the physics can be measured rather than only imagined. The same trick of wringing gain from engineered structure could feed work in optics, communications and quantum devices.

health & science

A gum-disease microbe is caught calcifying the heart's aortic valve5

In findings presented on July 13 at the American Heart Association's basic-science sessions, a Fuwai Hospital team in Beijing reported that Porphyromonas gingivalis, a bacterium behind gum disease, drives calcium buildup in the aortic valve by switching on the inflammatory protein IL-1 beta. In mice, repeated exposure worsened valve calcification and stenosis, while deleting IL-1 beta or giving preventive antibiotics blunted it. The work is preliminary and not yet peer-reviewed.

Why it matters: calcific aortic stenosis has no drug, only valve replacement, so a treatable trigger would be a real opening if it holds in people. The study puts a specific pathogen and a specific inflammatory switch behind the long-suspected link between the mouth and the heart.

wildcard

The deep ocean feeds its microbes by squeezing its own snow6

Writing in Science Advances on July 12, a University of Southern Denmark group showed that hydrostatic pressure in the deep sea wrings dissolved carbon and nitrogen out of sinking “marine snow,” the falling flecks of dead plankton and waste, much as a juicer does. Descending particles shed roughly half their carbon and about 60% of their nitrogen, and the freed nutrients made nearby bacteria multiply thirtyfold within two days.

Why it matters: it overturns the picture of the deep ocean as a starved desert, and it rewrites a term in the carbon budget. If pressure frees food in mid-water, less carbon survives the fall to be buried in the seabed, so models of the ocean's carbon sink may be counting on storage that never arrives.
