The Retry Loop You Can Delete

Constrained decoding masks the model's logits so it can only emit tokens a grammar allows, making invalid JSON structurally impossible; here is how the bitmask works, what it costs on real hardware, and why it does not hurt task accuracy.

7 min read2026-07-14

The pattern everyone writes, and the one that removes it

The standard way to get JSON out of a model is a loop: prompt for the schema, parse the reply, catch the parse error, retry. It mostly works, which is why it survives, but "mostly" is the problem. In one third-party benchmark on an H100, unconstrained decoding never got more than 72 percent of its outputs to satisfy a repetitive schema, while two guided backends hit 100 percent on the same model and prompt.1 Constrained decoding deletes the retry loop by making the invalid output impossible to emit in the first place.

Constrained decoding At each step the engine computes, from the grammar and the tokens emitted so far, the set of tokens that could legally come next, then sets the logits of every other token to negative infinity before sampling. The model still chooses, but only from tokens the grammar permits, so the finished string is guaranteed to parse.

This piece walks the mechanism from the outside in: the one-line way to turn it on in vLLM, the bitmask that makes it work underneath, the real overhead numbers with their baselines, and the evidence that structure does not cost you accuracy. The claim to keep in view is narrow and checkable: exact structural validity, at a per-token cost small enough that the throughput question depends on your serving stack, not on the idea.

Turning it on

In current vLLM the feature is a sampling parameter. You hand it a JSON schema, usually generated from a Pydantic model, and every token it returns is guaranteed to fit.2 One catch for readers on older builds: vLLM 0.12 renamed the guided decoding parameters, so guided_json and GuidedDecodingParams became structured_outputs and StructuredOutputsParams.3

That is the whole integration. What earns a systems desk's attention is the machinery a single parameter hides, because that machinery is where the cost lives.

The bitmask, from first principles

The engine compiles the grammar once into a pushdown automaton, a state machine that tracks how far into a valid string you are and which tokens may come next from each state.4 At every decode step it fills a bitmask with one bit per vocabulary entry, a legal token marked one and an illegal token zero, packed into an array of ceil(vocab / 32) 32-bit integers. It applies that mask to the logits in place, so masked tokens become negative infinity and vanish under the softmax, samples a token, and advances the automaton.5 The design goal is that the mask costs almost nothing per token, which is what a cached, precompiled automaton buys.6

Seen this way, constrained decoding is not a wrapper around the model but an edit to the sampling step. Nothing about the weights changes; the only new work is computing and applying the mask, and the engineering contest is about making that work vanish under the cost of the forward pass.

What it actually costs

The mask generation is fast. XGrammar's own measurements report its per-step logit masking is up to 3.5 times faster than prior grammar engines on JSON-schema workloads and more than ten times faster on general context-free grammars, against baselines including Outlines and the llama.cpp grammar engine.4 End to end, its authors report up to 14 times lower time-per-output-token on JSON-schema generation and up to 80 times on CFG-guided generation, with the gap widening as batch size grows.7 Read that 80 times as what it is: a large-batch, CFG-on-H100 figure, where CPU grammar processing would otherwise stall a fast GPU. It is not a number to quote for your JSON endpoint.

Two honest caveats sit next to the wins. First, the speedup is stack dependent. In the same third-party benchmark, vLLM's guided path shows a significant throughput drop once batch size reaches eight or more, while SGLang stays near flat with structured output on, so "constrained decoding is nearly free" is true on one engine and not the other.1 Second, constraints reduce malformed output without always eliminating it: on a hard dynamic schema, XGrammar still emitted 2.21 percent invalid JSON where a competing backend, LLGuidance, emitted 0.12 percent, so a grammar is a strong guarantee about token legality, not a promise that every complex schema is perfectly satisfied.1

The correctness gain, though, is not marginal, and it is the reason to adopt this at all. On a complex schema where unconstrained decoding produced structurally valid output only 61 percent of the time, guided decoding added twenty to twenty-five points of compliance; on an easier schema it lifted validity from the low nineties to the high nineties.1 If your pipeline currently pays for those failures in retries, the mask is buying that budget back.

Does forcing structure make the model dumber?

The persistent worry is that boxing the model into a schema degrades its reasoning. It traces to a 2024 paper, "Let Me Speak Freely," which reported accuracy drops when models were made to answer in a fixed format.8 The rebuttal is that the paper did not test constrained decoding. It measured JSON mode: the model was prompted to format its answer, the reasoning prompt was not held constant across conditions, and a separate model parsed the answer out, so any of those could carry the loss.

When the prompts are matched and the comparison is constrained decoding against free generation, the gap closes and often reverses. On three reasoning tasks the .txt team reported constrained generation tying or beating the unconstrained baseline: GSM8K went from 0.77 to 0.78, a last-letter task from 0.73 to 0.77, and a shuffled-object task from 0.41 to 0.44.9 The practical reading is that constraining the output format and constraining the reasoning are different acts. You can force valid JSON and still let the model think in a reasoning field inside that JSON, which is the pattern to reach for when you want both.

When to reach for it, and what it will not do

The rule that falls out is simple. If you need a machine-readable structure out of a model, constrained decoding is the default, not the optimization of last resort: it converts a probabilistic retry loop into a structural guarantee, and on a well-chosen stack it does so at a throughput cost near zero. Reach for it whenever a parse failure is a real cost in your system.

Two boundaries keep the decision honest. The grammar guarantees token legality, not semantic completeness: XGrammar does not enforce advanced JSON-schema keywords such as minLength, maxLength, minItems, or maxItems, so if your contract needs "between three and eight items" you still validate that yourself after decoding.2 And the backend is a real choice: vLLM ships more than one structured-output engine, and on high-concurrency dynamic-schema traffic the difference between them, in both throughput and residual invalid rate, is large enough to benchmark on your own workload before you commit.10 The mechanism is settled; the last mile is measuring it on the traffic you actually serve.
