Rabbit Holes

How to Lose Part of a Message and Read It Anyway

The polynomial algebra behind a scratched CD, a torn QR code, and Voyager's telemetry, and the decade it waited to become usable.

8 min read2026-07-11

A message with a hole in it

Print a company logo across the middle of a QR code, punch a hole through as much as thirty percent of it, and a phone still reads it without complaint. That is not tolerance for a smudge. It is exact recovery of the missing bytes, guaranteed by construction, at the highest of the four error-correction levels the QR standard defines.1

The same guarantee reads a CD through a scratch, reconstructs a file when two disks in a storage array die at once, and pulls telemetry out of Voyager from beyond the edge of the solar system. All of it rests on a five-page paper published in 1960 by two staff members at MIT Lincoln Laboratory, Irving Reed and Gustave Solomon, with a title that hides how far it would travel: "Polynomial Codes over Certain Finite Fields."2 The idea is worth taking apart, because once you see it you cannot unsee it in the machines around you.

Points on a curve

Start with the wasteful way to survive damage: send everything three times and take a majority vote. It works, and it triples what you transmit. Reed and Solomon found something far cheaper, and the whole scheme turns on a fact from school algebra.

Two points determine a line. Three points determine a parabola. In general, any k points with distinct positions fix exactly one polynomial of degree less than k that passes through them all. Reed and Solomon read that fact backwards. Take your k message symbols and treat them as the numbers that define such a polynomial. Then evaluate that polynomial not at k points but at n of them, with n larger than k, and transmit all n results.3 You have sent a curve, deliberately over-sampled.

Now the payoff. The curve was fixed by only k numbers, so any k of the n points you sent are enough to rebuild it, and from it the original message. The other n − k points are spare. Lose them, and it costs you nothing. A codeword carrying n − k extra points can afford to have n − k of its symbols simply vanish and still be read exactly, because whichever k survive still name the one curve that fits.

Erasure vs. error An erasure is a symbol you know is missing: its slot is blank. An error is a symbol that arrived looking perfectly valid but is wrong. The first tells you where the damage is; the second hides. That difference sets the price of recovery.

The hard case: errors that lie

Missing points are the easy problem. The real one is a point that arrived corrupted but plausible, a value that sits off the true curve while claiming to sit on it. You do not know which of the received points is the impostor. You have to find both where the errors are and what the right values were, using nothing but the points themselves.

This is why redundancy pays a different rate for errors than for erasures. To correct one error you need two spare symbols, not one: one to notice that a point is off the curve, another to pin down which point and by how much. So a code with n − k check symbols corrects up to (n − k) / 2 errors when their locations are unknown, exactly twice as many if you happen to know which symbols to throw out.3 The QR standard's own documentation states the same accounting from the user's side: correcting a given number of symbols costs twice that many code symbols.1

Reed–Solomon codes hit this trade-off at its theoretical ceiling. A code that adds n − k check symbols can do no better than guarantee that any two valid codewords differ in at least n − k + 1 places; that limit is the Singleton bound, and Reed–Solomon meets it exactly, which is what earns it the name maximum distance separable.3 There is no redundancy wasted. Every spare symbol buys the most error tolerance the mathematics allows.

One catch had to be handled for any of this to be exact. Evaluate an ordinary polynomial at large inputs and the numbers blow up; divide during reconstruction and you get fractions and rounding. So the arithmetic is not done on ordinary numbers but inside a finite field, a fixed, closed set of values where addition, multiplication, and division always land back inside the set with no rounding and no overflow.3 Choose a field of 256 elements and every symbol is exactly one byte. That is the quiet reason the codes fit real hardware: the algebra was built to close on the byte.

The idea outran the machinery

The 1960 paper defined the code and proved its properties. It did not give a practical way to decode the hard case. The obvious decoder tried every way of choosing which k of the n received symbols to trust and checked whether they agreed, which is fine for toy sizes and hopeless for real ones.3 For most of the 1960s the code was a beautiful result waiting for a method to use it.

The method arrived in 1969, when Elwyn Berlekamp and James Massey found an efficient decoder that locates the errors directly instead of guessing at the good symbols.3 That closed the gap between having the code and being able to run it. The first mass-market product to carry Reed–Solomon inside it did not ship until 1982.3 Twenty-two years from the theorem to the thing on the shelf.

One idea, wearing many disguises

The reach comes from how little the disguise matters. Change the field, the block length, and the interleaving, and the same points-on-a-curve idea reappears wherever bits have to survive something physical.

Voyager carried it into deep space. The 1977 mission used a Reed–Solomon code with a 255-symbol block containing 223 bytes of data over a field of 256 elements, able to correct any 16 corrupted bytes in a block, wrapped around an inner convolutional code.4 That pairing let the link reach a bit-error rate near one in a million at signal levels only a few decibels above noise.4 It has to: the transmitter on a deep-space probe radiates only a few watts, about the power of a dim light bulb, spread across the solar system.5

The compact disc took the harder engineering problem, which is that disc damage comes in bursts: a scratch wipes out a run of consecutive symbols at once. The fix, called cross-interleaved Reed–Solomon coding, uses two Reed–Solomon codes with a convolutional interleaver between them, so a physical burst gets scattered across many codewords, none of which sees more errors than it can fix. The result corrects a burst of up to about 4,000 bits, roughly 2.5 millimeters of track, and papers over gaps up to about 12,000 bits, some 7.5 millimeters, by interpolating the missing audio.6

The QR code you scan uses the same code with four selectable strengths, labeled L, M, Q, and H, that restore roughly 7, 15, 25, and 30 percent of the code's symbols respectively.1 Level H is why a logo can sit in the middle of one and the code still scans. Storage arrays lean on the same algebra: RAID-6 protects against any two drives failing at once by keeping two check quantities, an ordinary parity and a second that is a Reed–Solomon symbol computed over a field of 256 elements.7

The most telling reappearance is not error correction at all. In 1979 Adi Shamir published a scheme to split a secret into n shares so that any k of them reconstruct it and any k − 1 reveal nothing. His construction: hide the secret as one coefficient of a degree k − 1 polynomial, hand each participant one point on it, and recover the secret by the same interpolation, since any k points fix the curve.8 It is Reed–Solomon's exact machinery turned to the opposite purpose. Reed and Solomon over-sample a curve so a few lost points cannot erase the message; Shamir under-samples one so a few stolen points cannot reveal it. Same theorem, read from both ends.

Go deeper

The original paper is five pages and mostly readable to anyone comfortable with polynomials; read it for how compact the founding idea was, and notice what it does not contain, a fast decoder.2 Then trace the two threads it left open. For the mechanism, work a tiny example by hand over the field of 256 elements: encode a few bytes as a polynomial, evaluate them at distinct points, erase one, and interpolate it back; the whole scheme collapses into arithmetic you can do on paper.3 For the reach, read Shamir's three-page secret-sharing paper beside Peter Anvin's short note on the algebra of RAID-6, and watch the identical polynomial idea serve secrecy in one and durability in the other.87 The question worth carrying away is the one the 1960s answered slowly: how far a good idea can travel before the machinery to use it exists.
