Thomson Reuters v. Ross: The First Appeal on AI Training and Fair Use

A defunct legal-research startup's fight over headnote summaries is now the first case an appeals court has weighed on whether training an AI is fair use — and the whole thing turns on one factor.

10 min read2026-07-14
Thomson Reuters / West Publishing v. Ross Intelligence 3d Cir. · No. 25-2153
Stage
Interlocutory appeal from D. Del. No. 1:20-cv-613-SB; argued 11 June 2026, decision pending
Question
Are Westlaw's headnotes copyrightable, and was copying them to build a competing legal-research AI fair use?
Stakes
The first appellate reading of the fair-use factor every AI-training copyright case is fought on: effect on the market

On 11 June 2026 a three-judge panel of the Third Circuit sat in Philadelphia to hear argument in a copyright case whose defendant no longer exists.1 Ross Intelligence announced its own shutdown in December 2020, blaming the cost of this very lawsuit, and ceased operating a month later.2 What survives the company is the precedent: this is the first time a federal appeals court has weighed whether copying protected text to train an artificial-intelligence system can be fair use.3 Every larger suit now pending against OpenAI, Meta, Anthropic and the rest is arguing the same statutory test, and this is the first time it reaches an appellate bench.

The facts are almost quaint next to those cases — no chatbot, no scraped internet, no generated prose. Ross wanted to build a legal-research engine to compete with Westlaw, and to train it, it used Westlaw's editorial summaries of court decisions. A trial judge held that copying infringed and was not fair use. By the time you finish this piece you should be able to say what the court actually decided, why the case lives or dies on a single fair-use factor, why even the trial judge could not make up his mind, and how far a ruling either way would really reach.

What the district court actually held

On 11 February 2025, Judge Stephanos Bibas — a Third Circuit judge sitting by designation on the Delaware trial court — granted summary judgment to Thomson Reuters, finding that Ross had infringed 2,243 of Westlaw's headnotes and that its copying was not protected by fair use.4 Two things are worth separating immediately, because the headlines blur them: the court found the material copyrightable and found the copying unlawful. Neither is the sweeping "AI training is not fair use" ruling it is sometimes described as.

Headnotes and the Key Number System A headnote is a short editorial summary West writes for the top of a published opinion; the Key Number System is West's taxonomy that files each point of law under a numbered heading. Neither is the judge's own words — they are West's editorial layer on top of the public decision, which is why their originality is contested at all.

Fair use is a defence balanced across four statutory factors: the purpose and character of the use, the nature of the copyrighted work, the amount used, and the effect of the use on the market for the original.5 Bibas split the four. Factors two and three — the nature of the headnotes and the amount taken — went to Ross. Factors one and four — the purpose of the use and its market effect — went to Thomson Reuters, and the court held that factor four "matters more" than the others. Crucially, it found Ross's use was not transformative: the copying did not add new expression or meaning, it repurposed West's summaries to build a rival to West.4

The judge then drew a line around his own ruling that most coverage dropped. "Because the AI landscape is changing rapidly," he wrote, "I note for readers that only non-generative AI is before me today."4 Ross's system did not write anything; it returned quotations from actual judicial opinions in response to a legal question. That single sentence is the reason careful lawyers refuse to read this case as deciding the generative-AI suits — and the reason it is worth understanding on its own terms.

Why one factor carries the whole case

If you read only one part of the opinion, read the fourth factor. Under section 107, factor four asks about "the effect of the use upon the potential market for or value of the copyrighted work."5 The word doing the work is potential. Bibas held that it did not matter whether Thomson Reuters had itself built an AI product from the headnotes; the harm to a potential market for training data was enough, and it was Ross's burden to prove that market would not be affected.4

The court counted a market that may not yet exist, for a product the plaintiff had not built, as harm the defendant had to disprove.

That is the move on appeal. It has two edges an engineer should hold apart. The first is a market for the inputs: if training data can be licensed, then taking it without a licence displaces sales in a market for training data, whether or not the rightsholder is selling into it today. The second is subtler and more contested: Ross's outputs to a user were quotations from judicial opinions, which are not copyrightable at all, yet the court still treated the finished tool as a "market substitute" for Westlaw.4 Whether market harm can be built on outputs that themselves infringe nothing is precisely what the Third Circuit was pressed on, and the two sides were, in one advocate's telling, worlds apart on how to define the market at all.9

Thomson Reuters, and Judge Bibas below Training data is a licensable market, and a competing tool built from the material substitutes for it.

The opinion found "at least one potential derivative market is also obvious: data to train legal AIs," and that Ross built its product to compete directly with the source of that data — enough for factor four regardless of whether the model's outputs copied anything.4

Ross, and sceptical scholars You cannot count market harm from outputs that infringe nothing; the "dilution" theory does not fit here.

Because Ross's outputs were uncopyrightable judicial opinions, critics including Rebecca Tushnet argue the market-dilution reasoning imported from the generative-AI cases simply does not apply, and that weighing harm from non-infringing outputs is an error.6

Even the judge changed his mind

The strongest signal that this doctrine is unsettled is that the trial judge decided it twice, differently. Bibas had issued an earlier summary-judgment opinion in 2023 that sent the originality and fair-use questions toward a jury; on reconsideration he reversed himself on both, writing that his "prior opinion wrongly concluded that I had to send this factor to a jury" and that he was revising "all parts of my prior opinions that conflict with this one."4 Having changed his own answer, he then certified the two questions for immediate appeal under 28 U.S.C. § 1292(b), noting that "given the quickly growing importance of AI, the public will benefit from having the Third Circuit weigh in promptly."7

The UpCodes detail is the one to watch. Weeks before argument, the Third Circuit decided ASTM v. UpCodes, holding that republishing privately drafted technical standards once they are incorporated into law was likely fair use because the use was transformative under the Supreme Court's Warhol framework.8 The court then ordered Thomson Reuters and Ross to brief what impact that decision has on this appeal.1 The judge who wrote UpCodes, Luis Felipe Restrepo, also sits on the Ross panel — so the same court that just read transformativeness generously in one case now has to decide whether Ross's copying clears the same bar, on facts where the trial court said it does not.6

What an engineer should take from it

The temptation is to treat a ruling here as a verdict on AI training generally. It is not, and the reasons it is not are the levers any party in the bigger cases will pull to distinguish it — whichever way it comes out.6

  1. The system was not generative

    Bibas expressly limited his holding to non-generative AI; a model that produces new text is arguing a different transformativeness question than one that returns quotations.4

  2. The copied material was low-creativity, and the copier was a direct rival

    Editorial summaries used to build a head-on Westlaw competitor is close to the worst fact pattern for a fair-use defence; scraping varied web text to train a general model is not obviously the same.6

  3. Watch factor four, not the headline

    The operative question for your own corpus is whether there is a licensable market for the data you used, because that is where liability is being located — not in what the model learned.4

One scholar's summary of the asymmetry is worth keeping: a win for Thomson Reuters "will be an easy case for the pro-AI folks to cabin," because the facts are so specific to Westlaw and a direct competitor.6 The reverse is also true. Until the Third Circuit rules, the safest reading is the narrow one: this case is about a market for training data and a head-to-head competitor, and its reach beyond those facts is exactly what remains unsettled.

Go deeper

Start with the statute: 17 U.S.C. § 107 is a single paragraph, and reading the four factors once makes every one of these cases legible.5 Then read the primary record. Judge Bibas's February 2025 opinion is unusually clear for a copyright ruling and repays a full read, especially its factor-four discussion;4 the § 1292(b) certification opinion explains, in the judge's own words, why he thought the questions hard enough to send up.7 Read ASTM v. UpCodes alongside them to see how the same court handles transformativeness when it wants to.8 Then watch the Third Circuit docket for the opinion in No. 25-2153, expected later this year; when it lands, the factor-four holding is the first thing to read.1
