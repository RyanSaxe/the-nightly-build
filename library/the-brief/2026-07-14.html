The Brief: Who Keeps the Exhaust

As the model layer commoditizes on price, four of tonight's stories are really one story: the fight is moving to who owns the data underneath — trade secrets, enterprise exhaust, even ratepayer money.

5 min read2026-07-14
research · interpretability

Anthropic shows Claude's expressed values drift by model version and by language1

In work published on the 13th, Anthropic analysed 309,815 Claude.ai conversations from a two-week window in May across three model versions (Sonnet 4.6, Opus 4.6, Opus 4.7) and the platform's top 20 languages, compressing expressed behaviour onto four axes — Deference–Caution, Warmth–Rigor, Depth–Brevity, Candor–Execution — that explain about 15% of value variation once task and topic are controlled for. Opus 4.7 leans hardest to caution (0.24σ) and depth (0.23σ) while Sonnet 4.6 skews warm and brief; Claude expresses the most warmth in Hindi and Arabic, the most rigour in English and Russian.

Why it matters → This is the eval-reliability thread we have tracked turned inward: a frontier lab conceding that "behaviour" is a distribution that shifts by model version and even by language, not a fixed property you certify once. Alignment, like the agentic scores we flagged last week, is something you sample.

industry · funding

Nous Research, maker of the open Hermes agent, is in talks to raise at a $1.5B valuation2

The decentralised-training startup is raising at least $75M led by Robot Ventures at a $1.5bn valuation, a roughly 20x markup on the ~$70M it had raised to date, according to reporting on the 13th. The round is in talks, not closed — the number is a term sheet, not a bank balance.

Why it matters → A $1.5bn tag for an open-weight agent shop is a bet that the durable value sits in the agent and harness layer, not the base model — the same commoditisation logic driving the cost war, seen from the startup side.

industry · legal

Apple sues OpenAI, alleging trade-secret theft through ~400 ex-Apple hires3

Apple's 41-page complaint, filed on the 10th in the Northern District of California, alleges OpenAI recruited about 400 former Apple staff and names chief hardware figure Tang Yew Tan and an engineer who allegedly kept a work laptop and used an authentication bug to pull "dozens" of confidential files.4 OpenAI's reply: "We have no interest in other companies' trade secrets."

Why it matters → As OpenAI pushes into hardware, the binding constraint is not model quality but proprietary process knowledge. This is the sharpest version of tonight's theme — the contest has moved from who has the best model to who controls the data and IP that feed it.

compute · models

GPT-5.6 goes generally available on Amazon Bedrock, four days after launch5

AWS put the whole GPT-5.6 family — Sol, Terra and Luna — on Bedrock on the 13th at first-party pricing with a 90% cached-input discount. The posted flagship numbers: Sol scores 80 on the Artificial Analysis Coding Agent Index (2.8 ahead of the next model), 73.5% on ExploitBench against 47.9% for GPT-5.5, and 53.6 on Agents' Last Exam — all single-run and self-reported, so read them as vendor figures, not a harness.

Why it matters → The cost war we have been tracking goes multi-cloud: the same efficiency-per-token pitch behind GPT-5.6 and Grok 4.5 now lands inside AWS's commitment-and-caching billing, where price and availability, not raw capability, are the sell.

policy · energy

White House to press utilities and data centres to shield households from AI power costs6

Reuters reported on the 13th that the administration will convene utilities, data-centre developers and state governors "in the coming weeks" for a voluntary pledge that AI-driven demand will not raise household bills. It extends the March "Ratepayer Protection Pledge" — in which Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft, OpenAI, Oracle and xAI agreed to finance their own generation and grid upgrades rather than pass the cost to existing ratepayers.7 The event is not yet announced, and the pledge remains voluntary.

Why it matters → This is the political face of the compute build-out behind "Meta Compute" and the gigawatt data centres: the bill for the power crunch is landing on residential ratepayers, and Washington's answer is a pledge, not a mandate. Watch whether "voluntary" survives rising bills.

industry · strategy

Nadella warns enterprises they "pay for intelligence twice"8

In a weekend post, Microsoft's chief executive argued companies pay once in tokens and again by leaking proprietary knowledge — "trace by trace, correction by correction, eval by eval" — to the labs whose models they rent, and urged them to keep orchestration, memory and evaluations inside their own tenant boundary. The obvious tension, noted by reporters: Microsoft's own Copilot ingests the same enterprise data.

Why it matters → It is the clearest statement yet of the enterprise lock-in anxiety underneath the industry's "orchestration" manoeuvring, and it rhymes exactly with the Apple suit and Anthropic's values work: when the model is a commodity, whoever captures the data exhaust keeps the advantage.
