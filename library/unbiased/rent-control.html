Unbiased

One San Francisco study measured both the cost and the benefit of rent control

Mainstream economists read San Francisco's 1994 expansion as evidence that a rent cap shrinks the housing stock, while housing scholars read the same records as evidence that it keeps vulnerable tenants in place.

11 min read2026-07-19

A rent cap runs into the building it governs

A rent cap sets a ceiling on what a landlord may charge a sitting tenant, and every argument about it begins inside one building. When the market rent on a unit climbs above the capped rent, the difference is money the owner no longer collects, and the owner has choices: hold the tenant and absorb the gap, defer maintenance, convert the building to condominiums or owner-occupancy, or sell. The single study both camps reach for measured exactly this sequence. In 2019 the economists Rebecca Diamond, Timothy McQuade and Franklin Qian traced what happened after San Francisco's 1994 ballot measure extended rent control to small multifamily buildings, comparing treated buildings to nearly identical ones just past the cutoff.1 They found that rent control cut the rental supply from treated landlords by 15 percent, and that the same tenants it covered were 10 to 20 percent more likely to still live at their 1994 address years later. Both figures sit in one abstract, and each camp quotes one of them.

The question is not academic in 2026. In July 2024 the Biden administration proposed denying federal depreciation write-offs to corporate landlords holding more than fifty units unless they kept annual rent increases to 5 percent, a cap its own fact sheet said would cover more than 20 million units.2 California voters rejected Proposition 33, which would have repealed the state's Costa-Hawkins limits and let cities cap rents on any housing, including new construction; the state's nonpartisan analyst warned it would leave "fewer homes" available to rent,3 and the measure lost by roughly 62 percent, the third straight defeat for a California rent-control initiative after 2018 and 2020.4 St. Paul, which passed a strict 3 percent cap by ballot in 2021, has spent every year since softening it.5 On one side stand economists who argue the policy backfires on the renters it targets. On the other stand housing scholars who argue it protects them, and that the backfire is overstated.

A binding cap helps the tenant who holds a unit today by shrinking and misallocating the housing everyone who comes after her must rent, so the policy turns on its own intended beneficiaries over time.

Return to the San Francisco building. Diamond, McQuade and Qian found that treated landlords cut rental supply by 15 percent by converting units to owner-occupancy or condominiums and by redeveloping, and that the number of renters living in rent-controlled units fell 25 percent against 1994 levels.1 The withdrawn units did not reappear elsewhere; they left the rental market for good, and with less housing to rent, the market rent on everything still available rose. In the authors' own words, rent control "operated as a transfer between the future renters of San Francisco, who would pay these higher rents due to lower supply, to the renters living in San Francisco in 1994."1 Their verdict on the policy's aim was flat: it "appears rent control has actually contributed to the gentrification of San Francisco, the exact opposite of the policy's intended goal."1 Reviewing the wider literature, Diamond generalized the pattern: rent control "in the long run decreases affordability, fuels gentrification, and creates negative spillovers on the surrounding neighborhood."6

The spillover has been measured directly. When Cambridge, Massachusetts abruptly ended rent control in 1995, David Autor, Christopher Palmer and Parag Pathak tracked property values through decontrol. Decontrol added about $2.0 billion of the $7.7 billion in residential appreciation the city recorded from 1994 to 2004, but only $300 million of that came from the formerly controlled units themselves; $1.7 billion came from never-controlled buildings nearby that rose in value once the controlled units around them were freed.7 Put the other way, more than half, 55 percent, of the cost rent control had imposed fell on owners of housing the policy never touched.7 Control had been suppressing value across a whole neighborhood, not only inside the regulated units.

Even setting supply aside, a cap sends units to the wrong people. Edward Glaeser and Erzo Luttmer argued that the standard analysis misses the largest cost, because a controlled unit does not go to whoever values it most but to whoever arrived first and stays. Testing New York City's controlled stock, they found "an economically and statistically significant fraction of apartments appears to be misallocated across demographic subgroups,"8 the retired couple holding the family-sized apartment while the young family crowds into a studio. That misallocation is a welfare loss distinct from undersupply, and it grows the longer the cap holds tenants in place.

Asked directly, the profession answers with rare unity. When the University of Chicago's economist panel was polled in 2012 on whether local rent-control ordinances in cities like New York and San Francisco had improved "the amount and quality of broadly affordable rental housing," one panelist agreed and 37 did not.9 When the same panel was asked in 2024 whether the proposed 5 percent cap would make middle-income Americans better off over ten years, again one agreed; nineteen of the economists instead expected it to "substantially reduce the amount of available apartments."10

A cap does what it was built to do, keep vulnerable sitting tenants housed and undisplaced, and outside a few strong, hard-to-evade regimes the damage to the wider market is small or undetectable.

Start with the same San Francisco study, read for its other finding. Diamond, McQuade and Qian report that rent control's beneficiaries were "between 10 and 20% more likely to remain at their 1994 address," that the policy "lowers displacement from San Francisco," and that "the effects of rent control on tenants are stronger for racial minorities, suggesting rent control helped prevent minority displacement."1 The people the cap held in place were disproportionately those a hot market pushes out first. Manuel Pastor's team at USC, reading the same paper, tallied the transfer the economists described and drew the opposite bottom line: rent regulations "confer nearly $3 billion in benefits on incumbent renters," and while 42 percent of the offsetting welfare loss fell on future residents, "the net benefit for incumbent residents was positive."11 Those welfare magnitudes come from a structural companion model to the published study rather than its headline estimates, and Pastor's camp cites them to make its case; they are best read as the defenders' reckoning, not a separately audited fact.

The claim that caps choke construction, Pastor's review argues, does not hold for moderate regimes, because "newly produced units have no initial rent targets" and such rules "do not appear to decrease housing construction."11 The cleanest test of a moderate, long-standing regime is Joshua Ambrosius, John Gilderbloom and colleagues' study of New Jersey, where dozens of towns have run rent control with vacancy decontrol and hardship exemptions for four decades. Controlling for income and demographics, they found these forty-year-old policies "do not exert any statistically-significant effects on their communities' housing markets."12 The strong San Francisco and Cambridge results, on this reading, measured strong regimes and do not travel to the moderate caps most American cities actually run. Even Cambridge cuts two ways: where ending control lifted values, most of the gain flowed to owners of never-controlled property, not to any displaced tenant.7

The independent syntheses land in the same place. The Urban Institute's review of the literature concluded that "if rent control is judged on its ability to promote stability for people in rent-controlled units, evidence has generally found it to be successful," while research on rents, construction and conversion "have yielded mixed results."13 The stability effect is the settled finding; the market-harm effect is the contested one.

When New York strengthened its stabilization law in 2019, the NYU Furman Center looked for the predicted damage and, in the short run, did not find it. Its 2021 analysis reported "no evidence of an increase in HPD complaints after the passage of" the law, meaning no measurable rise in tenants reporting deteriorating conditions, and found eviction filings dropped around its passage before drifting back.14 The counterpart to displacement, on this evidence, was not a visibly crumbling housing stock.

The tenant in year one against the renter in year ten

The two camps are not mostly disagreeing about what happened. They are counting different people over different spans. The defenders' tenant is the incumbent of 1994, measured in the first years under the cap, while she is still in her apartment and has not been priced out; on that person, over that horizon, the policy plainly worked. The skeptics' renter is the newcomer of 2004 or 2026, measured after a decade of withdrawn supply has pushed market rents up; on that person, over that horizon, the policy plainly hurt. Diamond, McQuade and Qian measured both in one dataset, which is why both camps cite it honestly.1 Part of the split is a forecast, about whether the long-run supply loss eventually swamps the near-term protection, and part is a value choice, about which of the two renters a city owes more.

A second question is whether "rent control" even names one thing. The results that show large damage measured strong, hard-to-evade regimes: San Francisco's expansion and Cambridge's pre-1995 control with vacancy control. The results that show little measured moderate regimes with vacancy decontrol and new-construction carve-outs. St. Paul is running the conversion in real time: a flat 3 percent cap in 2021, a new-construction exemption and vacancy increases of up to 8 percent plus inflation by 2023, and a permanent exemption for buildings built after 2004 in 2025, leaving perhaps 90 percent of the stock still covered but the bite much reduced.5 Whether the design or the label governs the outcome is unsettled, and the same tension runs through the survey evidence: skeptics say the near-unanimous panel reflects theory plus the strong-regime findings, while defenders say it ran ahead of the messier evidence on moderate caps. Both readings fit the record.

The most-cited evidence that caps kill construction is St. Paul's building slump, and it is exactly where a causal claim should slow down. After the 2021 vote, St. Paul housing production fell 81 percent from its own three-year average. But rent-control-free Minneapolis, next door, fell 88 percent over the same window, as interest rates and construction costs hit both cities.15 The councilmember who led St. Paul's rollback blamed rates and costs and noted the ordinance was "the only thing that we can do anything about."15 Rent control may well have worsened St. Paul at the margin, but the macro shock did most of the damage in a city that had no cap at all, and the 81 percent figure cannot carry a clean causal story by itself.

The horizon cuts against the defenders too, and their own researchers are the ones who show it. The Furman Center that found no short-run degradation in 2021 reported in 2024 that in older, fully rent-stabilized Bronx buildings "the average unit was operating at an annual shortfall of $1,444," as regulated rents fell behind the cost of running the building.16 Same institution, opposite-looking findings, because they measure different years: the long-run maintenance decay the skeptics predict is now surfacing in the numbers of a pro-tenant research center rather than a landlord lobby.

The camp's own update
"Unless we vastly increase the amount of budget for subsidized housing, almost all of it is going to have to go to rescuing these buildings."16 Mark Willis, NYU Furman Center

What would settle the factual half is a study neither side has run at scale: follow the same population of vulnerable renters through both a capped and an uncapped regime over a full decade, and count both who stays housed and what the market rent does to the newcomer. In the near term the evidence will show up in three places: whether Furman's Bronx shortfalls spread to the rest of New York's stabilized stock, whether St. Paul's softened cap lets its permits recover in a way Minneapolis's do not, and whether any large public system reinstates a strong regime worth measuring. The value question, whose renter a city owes more, no dataset will settle. On it, the economists who built the strongest case against the policy offered an alternative rather than a rebuttal: if a city wants to insure tenants against rent spikes, Diamond and her coauthors wrote, it "may be less distortionary to offer this subsidy in the form of government subsidies or tax credits" than to freeze the price and let supply adjust.1 That keeps the 1994 tenant housed without asking the 2026 tenant to pay for it, and it is the rare place the mechanism the skeptics fear and the goal the defenders hold do not collide.
