Should the Fed Raise Rates, or Hold and Wait for Inflation to Fade?

Consumer prices are climbing at 4.2 percent a year while the core measure sits near 2.9, and heading into the Federal Reserve's July 29 meeting serious economists disagree on whether the gap is a passing energy shock or the start of something only tighter policy can stop.

8 min read2026-07-13

The number the Fed cannot agree how to read

In May, consumer prices rose 4.2 percent over the year, more than double the Fed's 2 percent goal. Strip out food and energy and the core index rose 2.9 percent; the gap is almost entirely energy, which climbed 23.5 percent over the same twelve months.1 On June 17 the Federal Open Market Committee held its benchmark rate in a range of 3.5 to 3.75 percent, its statement pinning the elevated numbers on "supply shocks that have driven price increases in certain sectors, including energy."2 What changed at that meeting was not the rate but the forecast. The committee's projection of where rates would end the year flipped toward tightening: nine officials now pencil in at least one hike in 2026, eight expect no change, and one a cut, and the panel raised its inflation projection to 3.3 percent for the year, up from 2.7 percent in March.3

The minutes of that meeting read as a committee arguing with itself. Participants "judged that the risks to the inflation outlook were still tilted to the upside." Many assessed that the appropriate rate "would be above the current target range at the end of this year," and a few went further, saying there was already "a case for raising the target range" even as they voted to hold.4 The new chair, Kevin Warsh, who inherited the job weeks earlier, has not tried to soften the message, and the July 29 meeting is his first defining call on rates. At the European Central Bank's forum in Sintra on July 1 he warned that anyone expecting the Fed to tolerate inflation above 2 percent would "be disappointed," and promised, "We're going to deliver price stability in the US."5

The neutral rate The interest rate that neither stimulates nor restrains the economy. When the funds rate sits above neutral, policy is tight and slows demand; below it, policy is loose and adds fuel. Much of the argument below is a disagreement about where neutral actually is, because that is what decides whether today's 3.5 to 3.75 percent is a brake or an accelerator.

The two camps do not dispute the arithmetic. They dispute what it means. One side sees a headline running above target, upside risks the Fed's own staff flag, and a central bank that has already spent years over its 2 percent goal; it wants rates higher. The other sees a core rate near target, a headline inflated by an energy spike no interest rate can undo, and an economy that is cooling on its own; it wants the Fed to wait. Both positions are held by serious, credentialed people, and each can be stated at full strength.

Inflation hawks and the committee's own hawkish bloc

With inflation still above target and its risks tilted up, policy is not actually tight; the Fed should raise rates now to keep expectations from slipping.

The strongest version of this case starts by questioning what "restrictive" means. Lawrence Summers argues the Fed has misjudged the neutral rate, placing it near 4.5 percent against the roughly 3 percent the Fed assumes. If he is right, a funds range of 3.5 to 3.75 percent is barely above neutral, which means a central bank that believes it is tapping the brakes is closer to pressing the accelerator, and is drifting toward another bout of inflation rather than away from one.6 He has circulated an updated version of a chart that lays today's inflation path against the 1979 episode, the last time a Fed convinced that price pressures were temporary let them harden into a decade-long problem.6

Forecasters who trade on the call are moving the same way. Bank of America, judging that "the Fed's inflation problem has gotten unambiguously worse," now expects three quarter-point hikes this year, lifting the benchmark to 4.25 to 4.5 percent by December.7 And the hawkishness is not only outside the building. The June minutes record a bloc of participants who see the appropriate rate ending the year above today's range, and a few who judged the case for raising was already made.4 The animating fear is credibility: after years above 2 percent, letting a fresh surge run risks un-anchoring the expectations that keep inflation contained, and "looking through" a supply shock is precisely the error that let prices accelerate in 2021.

Lawrence Summers, former Treasury Secretary and Harvard economist, standing here because he has been the most prominent public voice arguing US rates sit below neutral and must rise.6

Supply-shock economists who say the Fed is tight enough

Today's inflation comes from supply, not overheated demand, and rate hikes cannot fix supply; with policy already restrictive, the Fed should hold rather than damage a slowing economy.

Gregory Daco makes the case that the Fed is being asked to treat the wrong disease. Current pressures, he argues, are "not primarily driven by overheated demand" but by supply: the energy spike, and the strain artificial-intelligence buildout puts on power and semiconductors. Higher rates can discourage a buyer, but they cannot produce more oil, add generating capacity, or make chips appear. He reads monetary policy as already "slightly restrictive," and points to stalling real incomes, "a gradual erosion in spending power for many Americans," as evidence that demand is cooling without another turn of the screw.8

Even economists alert to the upside risks think the drivers pass. Jason Furman attributes the surge to tariffs, the Iran conflict, and data-center demand, "most of which are transitory," and sizes the first two at roughly half a point to a point of inflation each, the kind of level shift that fades from the annual figures once a year has passed.9 The core number supports the read: with energy stripped out, annual inflation is 2.9 percent, far nearer the target than the 4.2 percent headline the hawks cite, and much of that headline unwinds on its own if energy prices recede.1 Hiking into a supply shock, on this view, buys little disinflation because the shock does not respond to rates, while it does slow hiring and investment that do; the cost lands on the labor market and the benefit never arrives.

Gregory Daco, EY-Parthenon chief economist, standing here because he has argued in detail that rate hikes are the wrong instrument for supply-driven inflation and that the Fed should hold.8

Whether the energy spike washes out or seeps in

This is not a clash of values. Both camps want inflation back at 2 percent and both want to spare the job market; neither is defending a different goal. The disagreement is a question about the world, and behind it a forecast. Strip the labels and the two sides accept the same facts: a 4.2 percent headline, a 2.9 percent core, a 23.5 percent energy spike, a funds rate at 3.5 to 3.75 percent.1 What they cannot yet know is which way the numbers travel from here: whether the energy-and-tariff surge washes out and lets core drift down toward target, or seeps into wages and services and pulls core up.

That is an empirical question with a knowable answer, and the evidence is already starting to come in. The New York Fed's July survey of firms found that 47 percent of service companies and 44 percent of manufacturers that paid tariffs still have price increases to pass on, and it warns that a "one-time" price adjustment "may be a drawn-out affair, especially when the tariffs change frequently."10 That reading cuts toward the hawks, because it says the pass-through is not finished. Because a twelve-month inflation rate measures prices against a year earlier, a spike that does not repeat drops out of the annual figure as the calendar advances, even if prices never fall; that mechanical unwind is what the patience camp is counting on. Cutting the other way is energy: much of the 23.5 percent spike traces to the Middle East conflict, and if hostilities ease and oil retreats, the headline falls back toward the calmer core on its own, and patience is vindicated. The same data settle both questions, and they arrive on a schedule.

The first formal test is July 29, where Warsh has promised "a good family fight" and refused any forward guidance about which way it breaks.5 Beyond it lie the summer and autumn prints of core inflation, services prices, and wage growth, the series that reveal whether the surge is embedding. The committee's own median already leans toward tightening rather than easing by year-end, and it judges its preferred gauge, PCE inflation rather than the headline CPI, will still average 3.3 percent this year, above the 2 percent goal.3 And Warsh has drawn one line in advance, telling markets the Fed will stay independent and resist political pressure to cut, so the decision turns on the data rather than the White House.11 Whether the surge fades or embeds is a claim about the next several months of releases, and those releases will grade it.
