Should the world prohibit the development of superintelligent AI?

A cross-ideological coalition wants a halt until superintelligence is proven safe; the field's own pioneers cannot agree whether that is prudence or paralysis.

8 min read2026-07-11

A one-sentence demand that split the people who built the field

The sentence is short. "We call for a prohibition on the development of superintelligence, not lifted before there is broad scientific consensus that it will be done safely and controllably, and strong public buy-in."1 Since it appeared in October 2025, more than 70,000 people have signed it, among them five Nobel laureates and a politically incompatible crowd that runs from Steve Bannon to Prince Harry, from Obama's national security adviser Susan Rice to Apple's Steve Wozniak.2

What makes the fight worth the reader's minutes is who is on each side of it. Two of the researchers who built modern deep learning, Yoshua Bengio and Geoffrey Hinton, signed the call.2 The sharpest dissent comes from a peer of equal rank, Turing Award winner Yann LeCun, who calls the campaign to slow AI over extinction fears "extremely destructive."3 The question is not academic. In Geneva on 6 July, at the first UN Global Dialogue on AI Governance, the Secretary-General told every member state that "innovation needs guardrails" and framed the choice as "governing by design" against "drifting by default."4 The dialogue closed two days later with a promise to turn principles into enforceable practice before governments reconvene in New York in 2027, and with a warning from the Future of Life Institute's Jaan Tallinn that frontier labs keep shipping more capable systems "despite acknowledging significant safety challenges," with capability advancing quarterly and governance moving in years.5

Superintelligence Shorthand for an AI system that would surpass the best humans across essentially every cognitive task, rather than in a single domain. No such system exists; the fight is over whether to try to build one.

Strip away the celebrity signatures and the fight is a genuine one between serious people who understand the technology. One camp holds that a mind more capable than any human, built by people who admit they cannot yet control it, is a risk no society should run without consent. The other holds that a ban on a thing nobody can define is unenforceable, forfeits enormous benefits, and hands the future to whoever ignores it. Neither is the fringe caricature the other sometimes reaches for. Both cases follow, in the words of the people who make them best.

Safety scientists and an unlikely coalition

Do not build superintelligence until there is scientific consensus it can be done safely and public consent to run the risk.

The prohibition case does not rest on science fiction. It rests on an admission the builders themselves make: no one yet knows how to keep a system smarter than its makers under control. Bengio puts the burden where it belongs. "To safely advance toward superintelligence, we must scientifically determine how to design AI systems that are fundamentally incapable of harming people," he argues, and "the public has a much stronger say in decisions that will shape our collective future."2 Until that science exists, the camp says, the default should be restraint, not a race. The public appears to agree: in a poll released with the statement, 64 percent said superintelligence should not be built until it is proven safe, and only 5 percent backed today's unregulated development.2

The strongest version leans on the asymmetry of the mistake. Lord Browne, Britain's former defence secretary, writes that "there is no technical means in sight for humans to control AI systems more intelligent than ourselves," and that to build one anyway is to cede "control over the most powerful weapon the world has ever seen to the weapon itself."6 A nuclear weapon waits for a human hand. A superintelligence, the camp argues, need not. If they are right, the error is not one you get to correct, and no rate of return justifies running an experiment you cannot stop.

The camp is careful about what it is not saying. The demand is not "ban AI," which is why doctors, tools, and the models in daily use are untouched by it. It is a conditional halt on one specific endpoint, lifted the moment two conditions are met: a scientific consensus that control is solved, and public consent to proceed.1 The consent half matters as much as the safety half. Bengio's objection is partly democratic: a handful of firms are making a civilization-scale choice on everyone's behalf, and the public should "have a much stronger say in decisions that will shape our collective future."2

Yoshua Bengio — the world's most cited AI scientist, who chairs the International AI Safety Report written by more than 100 experts and backed by 29 nations, has standing here because the science of control is precisely his subject.7

Builders and techno-optimists

A prohibition on an undefined technology is unenforceable and self-defeating; safety is earned by building, and a halt only cedes the field.

The building case starts by attacking the demand itself. James Pethokoukis of the American Enterprise Institute argues that a standard of proven safety "often paralyzes progress," and that the statement's definition of harm is so broad it "effectively demands a permanent pause" no technology has ever cleared.8 Markets, not moratoria, discipline risk: firms that ship unsafe systems "face reputational collapse, lawsuits, and consumer flight."8 Marc Andreessen states the moral claim more bluntly, calling the precautionary principle "deeply immoral" and warning that "any deceleration of AI will cost lives" it might have saved.9

The strongest version concedes the danger and still rejects the ban. Leopold Aschenbrenner, a former OpenAI researcher, grants outright that "reliably controlling AI systems much smarter than we are is an unsolved technical problem," yet argues that "in the race to AGI, the free world's very survival will be at stake."10 A prohibition binds only those who honor it. Alibaba alone has committed roughly $53 billion over three years to AI models and infrastructure.11 A Western halt, this camp says, does not stop the technology; it just chooses who arrives first.

The camp is also careful about what it is not saying. It is not that the systems are harmless, but that the way to make a powerful technology safe is to build it, test it, and fix it in contact with reality, the way aviation and medicine earned their safety records. A permanent pause forfeits the gains too: Andreessen frames AI as a problem-solver whose absence has its own body count, arguing that "AI can save lives" in fields like medicine "if we let it."9 And the definition problem is not a quibble. Prohibit "systems that could be dangerous" and, Pethokoukis argues, you have written a rule that "effectively demands a permanent pause," because almost any capable model clears that bar.8

Yann LeCun — a Turing Award winner and one of deep learning's architects, has standing because he rejects the extinction premise the ban rests on as "extremely destructive," and warns that the CEOs hyping the danger "have a vested interest in propping up the power of the products they sell."3

The disagreement is a forecast, not a fact in hand

The two camps agree on more than the noise suggests. Both concede that controlling a system smarter than its makers is, today, unsolved: the accelerationist Aschenbrenner calls it "an unsolved technical problem,"10 and the prohibition camp builds its entire case on the same gap.6 What splits them is not that fact but two things that fact cannot settle. The first is a forecast: how soon does a system worth prohibiting actually arrive? One camp reads the timeline as short. Aschenbrenner calls AGI by 2027 "strikingly plausible,"10 and the prohibition signatories treat the window as closing fast enough to act now.5 LeCun reads the same evidence and calls the alarm overblown and "extremely destructive."3 The second is a value in tension: whether restraint before the fact lowers total risk or raises it by handing the lead to those who never restrain themselves.11

A fair reader can name the evidence that would move him, and roughly when it lands. On the forecast, the next two or three years of capability data decide it: if systems begin to automate their own research and cross clear superhuman thresholds, the prohibition camp's urgency is vindicated; if progress plateaus, LeCun's patience looks wiser. The neutral scoreboard already exists in the International AI Safety Report, a synthesis of the current evidence by more than 100 experts across 29 nations, chaired by Bengio himself.7 Read it and the same facts still support both "wait" and "proceed with care," which is the tell that the disagreement is not really about the evidence in hand. On the value question, the test is enforcement. If the 2027 UN session in New York produces a verification regime that binding commitments can rest on,5 the case for waiting gains force. If governance stays voluntary while labs and rival states accelerate, the unenforceability objection wins by default.

Three concrete signals are worth watching, because each side has effectively told us what would change its mind. The first is autonomous AI research: if models begin meaningfully improving their own successors, the timelines collapse and the prohibition camp's urgency stops being speculative. The second is the control ledger. If interpretability and oversight start to close the gap Aschenbrenner admits is open,10 the ban's premise weakens; if capability keeps outrunning control, it hardens. The place to read that ledger without a thumb on the scale is the same annual safety report Bengio chairs.7 The third is defection: whether a serious lab or state treats a pause as a press release while racing in private, which would answer the enforceability question the hard way. The honest position today is that no one holds the fact that decides it. That is what makes it a real question, and why the people who know the most disagree the most.
