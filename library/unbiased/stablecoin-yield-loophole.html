The Stablecoin Yield Loophole: Should Platforms Pay the Interest Issuers Cannot?

Congress barred stablecoin issuers from paying interest but said nothing about the exchanges that distribute them; as the GENIUS Act becomes rules this month, banks want that silence closed and the crypto industry wants it left open.

8 min read2026-07-14

A ban with a door left open

The GENIUS Act, the stablecoin law enacted in 2025, is explicit that a payment-stablecoin issuer "shall not pay" any interest or yield to a holder simply for holding the coin.1 It says nothing about the exchanges and platforms that distribute those coins. That silence is the whole fight. An issuer cannot pay you to hold its dollar token, but Coinbase or another platform can hand you a "reward" that, to the person receiving it, looks and spends exactly like interest.

Why now The question is live because the law is turning into rules this month. Treasury opened the illicit-finance rulemaking in April 2026, and the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency and Treasury are drafting the final GENIUS rules on a mid-2026 timetable, the moment at which the statute's silence on platform rewards is either closed or left standing.2

Both sides are already in the room. The American Bankers Association and more than fifty state associations have urged the OCC to read the statute as requiring a broad prohibition that reaches the platforms, not only the issuers.3 The crypto industry treats any move to reopen the bargain struck in GENIUS as a line it will not let regulators cross. What follows is the strongest version of each case, in the words of the people making it.

The banks and their regulators

Congress banned yield on stablecoins to protect the insured-deposit base that funds lending; letting platforms route economically identical rewards to holders reopens that ban through the back door and will drain deposits and shrink credit.

The banks' case begins with what a deposit does. A bank deposit is a regulated liability, recycled into supervised lending and disciplined by the loans a bank can make against it. An exchange paying rewards, on the industry's own account funded through an issuer's distribution arrangements, does no comparable regulated lending, so a dollar that leaves a bank for a yield-bearing stablecoin does not return to the same town as a small-business loan. On that reasoning the American Bankers Association warns that platform rewards are a pass-through evasion of a limit Congress wrote on purpose.4

The scale of the worry is what makes it more than a turf complaint. The Bank Policy Institute's Bill Nelson argues that yield-bearing stablecoins would not merely move deposits between banks but push them out of the banking system: "Once the dust settles and interest rates and balance sheets adjust, deposits will decline and so will bank lending," a channel he sizes, at wide adoption, at trillions of dollars of deposits and a double-digit percentage cut to lending.5 Community banks, which fund local credit from exactly this deposit base, are the institutions the camp says would feel it first.

There is a design argument underneath the forecast. Deposits are cheap to a bank precisely because they are insured and sticky, and that cheap, stable funding is what lets a bank lend long against short liabilities. The banks read the GENIUS issuer ban as Congress deliberately protecting that arrangement rather than as an oversight, and a platform reward that mimics interest as exactly the thing the ban was written to prevent.1

The remedy the camp presses is not new legislation but a reading of the one just passed. In a joint letter, the banking groups argue the OCC "should issue a broad prohibition because Congress required one," treating the issuer ban as meaningless if an affiliated platform can pay the same reward the issuer cannot.6 The banks stress that the prohibition they seek is the OCC's to write under authority it already has, so no new act of Congress and no reopening of the compromise is required.3 Close the door, on this view, and you simply enforce the bargain the statute already struck.

Rob Nichols, American Bankers Association, standing here because he leads the trade group driving the case to the OCC and has put the deposit-flight warning under his own name: the proposal "would unnecessarily incentivize the flight of bank deposits into payment stablecoins, putting both economic growth and financial stability at risk."4

Coinbase and the crypto industry

Banks pocket the spread between what safe assets earn and the near-zero they pay savers; letting stablecoin holders earn a market return is pro-consumer competition, and banning it is protectionism dressed as safety.

The industry's case is framed as fairness to the holder. A stablecoin is backed by short-term Treasuries and cash that earn a real return; the question is who keeps it. Coinbase's Brian Armstrong argues the saver should share in it, and that the state has no business preventing that: crypto companies "should be allowed to, and incentivized to, share interest with consumers," and "the government shouldn't put its thumb on the scale to benefit one industry over another."7 On this reading the bank objection is not about safety but about a spread the banks would rather keep.

The competition argument is the camp's core. Money-market funds and Treasury bills already let sophisticated savers earn close to the safe rate; a yield-bearing stablecoin, the industry says, extends that access to anyone with a phone, and a rule forbidding it protects bank net-interest margins rather than consumers. Armstrong's framing is that the state is choosing an incumbent industry over savers when it bars the pass-through.7

The camp also rejects the framing of a "loophole" to be closed. Armstrong treats any effort to reopen the statute as off the table, writing that the industry "won't let anyone reopen $GENIUS."8 And it casts the deposit-flight campaign as incumbents lobbying to freeze a more competitive product out; Senator Bernie Moreno, on the Senate Banking Committee, put the industry's read of the bank push bluntly: "The banking cartel is in full panic mode."9

Not every voice on this side wants unlimited passive yield. Circle's Jeremy Allaire offers the industry's own limiting principle, a reward tied to use rather than to mere holding: "It has to be based on real transactions, real payments volume, real activities, and that's exactly the kind of incentivization that we want to see."10 That compromise concedes the banks' disintermediation fear enough to route around it, while keeping the consumer upside the camp is unwilling to give up. It also exposes a split within the industry: a rewards-for-activity model is a smaller prize than the passive market rate a holder could otherwise collect, which is why the purest version of the consumer-yield case resists even that limit.10

Brian Armstrong, Coinbase, standing here because he runs the largest US exchange with the most at stake in the rule and has argued the consumer-yield case, and the refusal to reopen GENIUS, under his own name.7

The one number the whole fight is waiting on

Strip the rhetoric and the sides agree on the mechanics. Issuers cannot pay yield; platforms, for now, can; the rulemaking will decide whether that gap closes. What they dispute is a forecast, and it reduces to a single empirical question: would letting holders earn yield genuinely drain insured deposits and cut lending to households and small businesses, or would it mostly transfer the interest spread from banks to consumers while deposits recycle back through the system?

The dueling studies are the fight in miniature. The Bank Policy Institute's estimate is the maximal one: deposits and lending "destroyed" at the trillions scale once balance sheets adjust.5 The Federal Reserve's own analysis is conditional and far more restrained. In a December 2025 staff note, Jessie Jiaxu Wang concludes that stablecoins "can either reduce, recycle, or restructure bank deposits rather than simply draining them," with the hit to credit ranging from roughly sixty-five billion dollars to about 1.26 trillion depending on how widely they are adopted and how issuers hold their reserves.11 The gap between "1.26 trillion at the high end, conditional" and "trillions, destroyed" is the space the two camps are really arguing in.

The mechanism under the disagreement is where the deposits actually go. The Fed's "recycle" case is that a dollar moved into a stablecoin largely returns to the banking system as a deposit the issuer holds against its reserves, so the funding survives even as it shifts between institutions.11 The Bank Policy Institute's "destroy" case is that issuers park reserves in Treasuries and at the central bank instead, so the funding leaves the deposit base that supports lending altogether.5 Which behavior dominates is an empirical question about how issuers hold reserves, and the rule being written now will partly determine the answer.

Beneath the forecast sits a values question the numbers cannot settle: whether it is the government's job to protect a deposit-funded lending model that channels savings into local credit, or to let consumers capture a market return in open competition and let the funding model adapt. A reader who thinks the first is a public good will read the banks' prohibition as prudence; a reader who thinks the second is a consumer right will read it as capture.

What would move a fair reader is evidence not yet in, and it arrives on a knowable schedule. The near-term signal is the rule itself: whether the OCC and Treasury read GENIUS to reach platform rewards, or leave the door open and let the market test the deposit-flight thesis in real time. The longer signal is the deposit data at community banks in the quarters after, and whether any observed outflow recycles back into the system as the Fed's note allows or drains out of it as the Bank Policy Institute predicts. The politics compress that timeline: with the rules due this month and the same yield question already fought through Congress, the decision will likely be made before the deposit data that would settle it exists. Until one of those signals lands, the same statute can be read, in good faith, as a consumer protection wrongly withheld or a guardrail about to be evaded.
