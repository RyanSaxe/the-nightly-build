Word of the Day

The work tells the next worker what to do

Pierre-Paul Grassé built the word in 1959 to explain how a termite colony rebuilds its nest with no plan and no foreman, each worker guided by the work already in front of it.

5 min read2026-07-24

A nest that rises with no one holding the plan

A worker termite reaches a half-built wall, sets down its load of mud, and leaves. No termite told it where to build. The wall it arrived at was its whole instruction, and the wall it leaves is the instruction for whoever comes next. Out of thousands of such deposits, with nothing supervising them, the broken nest is rebuilt, whole and correct.

The word for this is stigmergy: coordination that runs through the traces left in a shared thing being worked on, not through messages between the workers.1 The work already done stimulates the next act, real coordination with no coordinator.

What Grassé could not find between the workers

The French zoologist Pierre-Paul Grassé coined stigmergie in 1959, in a paper on how colonies of Bellicositermes natalensis and Cubitermes rebuild a nest broken open.2 What he could not find in the rubble was the thing every account of insect building assumed: communication between the workers. No worker gave orders or could see enough of the nest to hold a plan, yet the repair proceeded correctly. This is the coordination paradox: how insects with no apparent communication build complex things together.1

Grassé's definition, from the English abstract of his 1959 paper
…the stimulation of workers by the very performances they have achieved.1 Pierre-Paul Grassé (1959), quoted by Francis Heylighen

The worker does not direct the work; the work directs the worker. Grassé's French original of the line has not been recovered. It survives only in his English abstract.

A word cut from Greek for a blind builder

Grassé cut the word to fit the observation. It joins the Greek stigma (στίγμα), a mark or a puncture, to ergon (ἔργον), work or its product.3 Read tidily, the work leaves a mark the next worker reads. Grassé meant it sharper, taking stigma as a goad, the piqûre of his French, so the work already done pricks the next worker into acting.1

The clean picture, that the shape of the work speaks, does not hold for the termite: it is blind. What cues the next deposit is not the mud's shape but a pheromone the earlier workers mixed into it.1 Sematectonic coordination works through the shape of what was built; marker-based coordination works through a signal laid on it, like a scent, doing no structural work of its own.4 Grassé's founding example, read strictly, is the marker-based kind wearing the sematectonic story.

The trail becomes a number a program can read

The same idea became an algorithm once the trace could be counted. Jean-Louis Deneubourg's team showed Argentine ants self-organizing a trail out of random wandering, on trail-laying and trail-following alone.5 Marco Dorigo made that loop into search: on a double bridge the shorter path wins, walked more often so its trail builds faster and draws still more ants, his virtual ants depositing a number on each graph edge that evaporates over time and biases the next toward the busier edges.6

The coordination arrived before the word did. Dorigo's 1996 paper never once says stigmergy. It calls the loop autocatalytic and speaks of pheromone.6 The label was fastened onto ant algorithms only later, by Dorigo with Bonabeau and Theraulaz in 2000.1 Engineers had rebuilt Grassé's idea from scratch and named it after him afterward, narrowing it to a quantity, a scent to be reinforced.

When the artifact itself carries the message

The same shape appears wherever people leave their work where the next person will act on it. A Wikipedia article's current state, or a codebase's, tells the next contributor what is missing, and the work grows because each person builds on what the last one left.1 The plainest case is the desire-path, the strip of dead grass worn across a lawn: the record of everyone who took the shortcut, and the instruction the next walker follows.4

These human uses keep Grassé's core, coordination through the shared thing not through talk, and loosen his strict claim: a walker can see the whole path and choose against it. The desire-path, though, is the clean sematectonic case the termite only pretended to be. Nothing but the worn shape of the ground carries the message, no scent added on top.4

What only stigmergy names

Two bigger words sit next to stigmergy. Self-organization is the whole family of order arising without a central controller; stigmergy is one case inside it, where coordination runs through traces left in a shared medium.1 A flock steering by watching its neighbors is self-organizing but not stigmergic, since it leaves no trace to read. Emergence names the outcome, the structure no single part contains; stigmergy names the mechanism that produces it.1 Calling a termite cathedral emergent describes the result; calling it stigmergic says how it got built.

The word also misleads. It is not coordination under a plan or a boss, whose orders are the direct communication stigmergy does without.7 And it is not only the ant-colony trick. Heylighen warns that the commonest error shrinks it to virtual pheromone reinforced by virtual ants, when it needs neither markers nor quantities and works even for one person editing a draft.1

What the word marks off is a particular kind of order. The signal from each worker to the next is the changed state of the thing they all work on, and the half-built work is the only instruction anyone consults. It is enough.
