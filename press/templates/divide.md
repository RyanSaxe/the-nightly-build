# divide — editorial brief

The registry entry fixes this template's shape: two sides, rendered left versus
right, each cited, with a crux and a what-would-change-my-mind. This brief gives
it its identity. It composes as a layer and never overrides the house floor.

A divide is not a both-sides summary. Its job is to make a genuine disagreement
legible by arguing each side at its strongest, then locating exactly where the two
part company. Weak divides restate two positions and stop; strong ones do the
analytical work of showing why smart people land on each side and what evidence
each is reasoning from.

Each side steelmans a real position: the case a serious proponent would actually
make, grounded in what they have said and the data they cite, not a strawman built
to lose. The crux is the heart of the piece. It names the specific fact,
assumption, or value where the sides truly diverge, the thing that, if resolved,
would move a reasonable person. Do not settle for "it depends." Say what it depends
on.

what-would-change-my-mind keeps the piece honest: the concrete, checkable
observation that would push the crux one way or the other. If nothing could, the
disagreement is not empirical, and say so.

Depth over symmetry. Go as deep as the material demands within each side; the two
columns need not be equal length, only equally serious.
