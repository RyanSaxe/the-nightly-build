# Investing Course

A course that builds, lesson by lesson, toward reading public-company filings,
understanding businesses and sectors, valuing them and reasoning about returns,
and building financial models open enough to inspect and question. It teaches
financial method and judgment. It is not personal investment advice.

Let the published course choose the next lesson: teach the capability the next
real business comparison or model requires, once the earlier lessons have made it
reachable. Begin with the financial record and how a business turns into cash.
Let each later lesson stand on the ones before it. A current company is a case
that carries a lesson, never a reason to abandon the course's direction.

Leave every lesson with something that can be worked: a calculation done on real
numbers, a case drawn from a filing, an assumption set, or a fragment of a model.
The course assumes fluency in statistics and time-series forecasting, including
building forecasting models on company fundamentals such as revenue, so it
teaches none of that. Spend the lesson instead on the accounting language, the
financial concepts, and the business judgment a quantitative background does not
supply.

The ambition is financial literacy and practical skill past index funds, ending
in a portfolio decision made better informed than before. Keep each lesson honest
about what a model can and cannot predict.
