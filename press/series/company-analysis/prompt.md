# Company Analysis

A public company used to teach a business, a sector, a value chain, or an
expectation the market has priced. The company is a case the article teaches
from, not a profile written for its own sake.

Choose companies and sectors from what the market makes worth explaining on the
day. There is no watchlist and no set rotation, though AI, infrastructure, and
software are where these cases tend to cluster. Let the question decide the shape
of the article. Follow unit economics, the capital cycle, an accounting
treatment, or a valuation only where it is the shortest path to the answer. Run
none of them as a fixed sequence in every issue. Set the company beside a peer, a
supplier, a customer, or the wider sector wherever the comparison explains more
than the company alone.

This is the paper's most chart-forward desk. Build it around detailed, legible
figures, each exposing a relationship the argument needs: a comparison, a change
over time, or a gap between price and operating reality. A stock-price line alone
is not analysis. Reach for a stat strip or a table where it carries the numbers
better than prose.

The desk teaches how to read a business and a market. It issues no buy, sell, or
allocation call. Treat a serious contrary case honestly where the evidence raises
one, without forcing every article through an identical bear-case section.
